# /lenny-negotiation-comms - Negotiation & Communication

Master the human side of product work — from negotiating your comp and communicating tradeoffs to giving hard feedback, building influence without authority, and navigating difficult conversations that most people avoid.

## About This Journey
Product leaders live and die by their ability to communicate, influence, and negotiate — yet most never receive training in any of these. This journey connects the frameworks of Jacob Warwick (GAINS negotiation), Jack Cohen (GAIN feedback), Jules Walter (PM influence tactics), Wes Kao (managing up and clarity), Alisa Cohn (scripts for difficult conversations), Tara Seshan (communicating tradeoffs), Rachel Lockett (active listening and coaching), Jeffrey Pfeffer (7 Rules of Power), and Lenny's own hard-won lessons on communicating bad news. You'll build a sequential toolkit: first understand how influence actually works, then learn to communicate with precision, then master giving and receiving feedback, then handle the hardest conversations, and finally negotiate your own value with confidence.

Best for: PMs, engineering managers, and product leaders navigating raises, cross-functional conflicts, exec communication, performance feedback, or anyone who knows they need to get better at the human side of building products but has been avoiding it.

5. **Negotiating Your Value** — Niya Dragova's 10 Commandments of salary negotiation, Jacob Warwick's GAINS framework (Gather, Align, Influence, Navigate, Secure) for comp negotiations, and Phyl Terry's candidate market fit concept for playing to win. *(Sources: "The 10 commandments of salary negotiation", "The ultimate guide to negotiating your comp", Phyl Terry — Land your dream job)*

## Coaching Session Instructions

When this skill is invoked, follow this exact flow:

### Opening
1. Give a brief, motivating overview: the human side of product work — influence, communication, feedback, and negotiation — is the highest-leverage skill set most PMs never train. These aren't soft skills; they're the skills that determine whether your ideas ship, your team thrives, and your career compounds. 3-4 sentences, make the user feel excited.
2. Show the roadmap: "We'll cover 5 concepts that build on each other. Here's the journey: (1) How Influence Actually Works, (2) Communicating with Precision, (3) The Art of Feedback, (4) Scripts for Difficult Conversations, (5) Negotiating Your Value. By the end, you'll have a practical toolkit for every high-stakes human interaction in your career."
3. Ask: "What's the conversation you're most dreading or avoiding right now? It could be a comp negotiation, a tough feedback conversation with a report, pushing back on a stakeholder, communicating a project failure to leadership — whatever is top of mind. Also, what's your role and seniority level so I can tailor examples?"
4. Wait for their answer. Use it to personalize the entire session.

### For Each Concept (walk through in order, one at a time)

#### Concept 1: How Influence Actually Works
1. **Load the source:** Use the Lenny MCP's `read_content` tool with filename `newsletters/how-to-get-better-at-influence.md` to get the full article. (In Claude Code this is `mcp__lennysdata__read_content`; other tools may name it differently — use whatever MCP tool reads content from the lennysdata server.) Then also load `newsletters/a-pms-guide-to-influence.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's seven influence strategies using the Frodo Baggins framework — make their goals your goals, charge your trust battery (Tobi Lütke's concept), help them see what you see, show success, bring evidence, leverage authority, be likable. Then layer Jules Walter's five tactical moves: seek intel on how each stakeholder makes decisions, frame your message from their POV, prime detractors and champions in the "meeting before the meeting," make people feel heard and validated, and manage the clock.
3. **Apply it:** Connect to the user's situation: "For your situation, this means..."
4. **Check understanding:** Ask the user to explain the difference between making their goals your goals vs. framing from their POV — these are related but distinct moves. Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If vague, clarify: "Making their goals your goals is about *aligning incentives* before the ask. Framing from their POV is about *translating your proposal* into their language during the ask."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 1 of 5. Next up: how to communicate with precision — especially tradeoffs and bad news."

#### Concept 2: Communicating with Precision
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/how-to-communicate-tradeoffs-so-leaders-will-listen.md`. Then also load `podcasts/wes-kao.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Tara Seshan's five principles — repetition doesn't spoil the prayer (use an Ongoing Stack Rank), steelman the request before the meeting, frame company-first not team-first, predict the future a little bit, and always communicate an opinionated decision using SCQA (Situation, Complication, Question, Answer). Layer in Wes Kao's managing-up framework and her insight that most people spend too much time on the "what" and "why" and not enough on the "super specific how." For bad news, share Lenny's five rules: get ahead of it, highlight the good, share the context, have a crystal clear POV on what to do next, and separate yourself from your projects.
3. **Apply it:** "For your situation, this means..."
4. **Check understanding:** Ask: "If your CEO just dropped a high-priority feature request on your team's roadmap, walk me through the first three things you'd do before responding." Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** The key moves are: (1) steelman the request by talking to the requester with curiosity, (2) check it against your Ongoing Stack Rank, (3) frame the tradeoff using company goals, not team goals.
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 2 of 5. Next: the art of giving feedback that people actually thank you for."

#### Concept 3: The Art of Feedback
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/introducing-the-gain-framework-for-feedback-an-evidence-based-approach-to-giving.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Jack Cohen's GAIN framework — Goal (what both parties want more of, "the dream within the conflict"), Actions (observable behaviors, not judgments), Impacts (the effects of those actions), Next actions (who will do what by when). The key research insight: Gottman can predict divorce with 93% accuracy based on how couples open conflict conversations. Couples who share "the dream within the conflict" — what they want more of — stay together. The same applies at work. Frame feedback as "gain" not "pain." Share the Laura/micromanaging founder example as the model case.
3. **Apply it:** "For your situation, this means..."
4. **Check understanding:** Ask: "Think of a piece of feedback you need to give someone right now. Draft the opening line using the GAIN framework — start with the Goal, what you both want more of." Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If they start with a judgment ("You need to stop being...") or a complaint, redirect: "That's a judgment, not an observation. As Jack Cohen puts it, 'feedback is creating visibility into people's actions and their impacts.' Try again: what specific action did you observe, and what was its impact?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 3 of 5. Next: the exact scripts for the conversations you've been avoiding."

#### Concept 4: Scripts for Difficult Conversations
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `podcasts/alisa-cohn.md`. Then also load `podcasts/rachel-lockett.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Alisa Cohn's approach — start with observable facts, not judgments. Her script formula: "I want to chat with you about [topic]. What I'm hearing/observing is [specific data]. We both know that [shared goal]. I want to hear what you have to say, and the most important thing is that we leave knowing [concrete next step]." For when someone gets defensive: pause, reaffirm why you're having the conversation ("I'm telling you this because I know how important your career is to you"), offer to continue or pause. Then share Rachel Lockett's key insight: "The goal of any conflict is to create mutual understanding, not to convince the other person that what they're doing is wrong." Her three levels of listening: Level 1 (internal — thinking about yourself), Level 2 (focused — repeating their words), Level 3 (global — hearing what they're communicating beneath the words, noticing tone and body language).
3. **Apply it:** "For your situation, this means..."
4. **Check understanding:** Ask: "Using Alisa's formula, script the opening 2-3 sentences for a difficult conversation you need to have. Say it like you'd actually say it." Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** Check that they included observable facts (not judgments), a shared goal, and a concrete desired outcome. If missing any, coach them to add it.
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 4 of 5. Last one: negotiating your own value — comp, promotions, and career leverage."

#### Concept 5: Negotiating Your Value
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/the-ultimate-guide-to-negotiating-your-comp.md`. Then also load `newsletters/the-10-commandments-of-salary-negotiation.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Start with Niya Dragova's mindset shift — "everyone else is playing this game, and you're probably losing." Her 10 Commandments: negotiation starts earlier than you think, mine for intel during interviews, don't give in to recruiter pressure to name a number, at FAANG the recruiter may have no say (compensation committee), read between the lines of your initial offer, at startups the playbook is different, your job is to win hearts and minds, get good data, compare offers holistically, and negotiate over the phone not email. Then layer Jacob Warwick's GAINS framework: Gather intelligence (organization dynamics, decision-maker psychology, strategic pain points), Align through storytelling and "selling the vacation," Influence by building champions across functions, Navigate complexity with strategic patience, Secure by engineering agreements and defining vague terms. As Warwick puts it: "Instead of asking 'What can you offer me?', demonstrate 'Here's how I'll solve your most pressing challenges.'"
3. **Apply it:** "For your situation, this means..."
4. **Check understanding:** Ask: "What's the single biggest mistake most people make when negotiating comp, according to what we just covered?" Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** The core mistake is selling credentials instead of solving their problems — positioning yourself as an applicant rather than a peer. As Niya Dragova puts it, "instead of asking for money, make them give you more money by bringing in obstacles the recruiter needs to overcome to close you."
6. **Signpost:** "That's all 5 concepts. Let's bring it all together."

### Closing
1. Ask the user for a 30-second summary of everything they learned, as if explaining to a friend who's about to walk into a tough meeting.
2. Give 3 concrete next steps — specific, time-bound, doable this week:

3. Offer to save session notes. Ask the user where they'd like to save. Suggest `./lenny-sessions/[YYYY-MM-DD]-negotiation-comms.md` as default.

## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet

- **Seven Influence Strategies** (Lenny Rachitsky): Make their goals your goals, charge your trust battery, help them see what you see, show success, bring evidence, leverage authority, be likable.
- **Five Influence Tactics for PMs** (Jules Walter): Seek intel on stakeholder decision-making, frame from their POV, prime detractors and champions in the "meeting before the meeting," make people feel heard and validated, manage the clock.
- **Steelmanning** (Tara Seshan): Before pushing back on a request, present the strongest possible version of the requester's argument — it makes you more persuasive and eliminates surprises in exec meetings.
- **SCQA** (Tara Seshan via Barbara Minto): Situation, Complication, Question, Answer — the structure for communicating an opinionated decision to leadership. Always put the decision (BLUF) at the top.
- **Ongoing Stack Rank (OSR)** (Tara Seshan): A public, continuously updated priority list that changes the conversation from "Why can't we do this?" to "Do you agree that X is more important than Y?"
- **GAIN Feedback Framework** (Jack Cohen): Goal (what we both want more of), Actions (observable behaviors, not judgments), Impacts (effects of those actions), Next actions (who does what by when). Gain-framing beats pain-framing — Gottman's research shows couples who share "the dream within the conflict" stay together.
- **Three Levels of Listening** (Rachel Lockett): Level 1 (internal — thinking about yourself), Level 2 (focused — repeating back their words), Level 3 (global — hearing beneath the words, noticing tone, body language, context).
- **Difficult Conversation Script Formula** (Alisa Cohn): "I want to chat with you about [topic]. What I'm observing is [specific facts]. We both know [shared goal]. Let's leave this knowing [concrete next step]." When they get defensive: pause, reaffirm intent, offer to continue or reschedule.
- **10 Commandments of Salary Negotiation** (Niya Dragova): Never name a number first, mine for intel during interviews, negotiate over the phone not email, you don't need a competing offer, and instead of asking for money — make them give you more by surfacing obstacles they need to overcome to close you.
- **GAINS Negotiation Framework** (Jacob Warwick): Gather intelligence others miss, Align with advocates through storytelling and "selling the vacation," Influence momentum by adding value, Navigate complexity with strategic patience, Secure success by engineering agreements and defining vague terms.
- **7 Rules of Power** (Jeffrey Pfeffer): Get out of your own way, break the rules, appear powerful, build a powerful brand, network relentlessly, use your power, and success excuses almost everything. As Pfeffer puts it: "This is not about personality. These are skills that can be mastered."
- **Five Tips for Communicating Bad News** (Lenny Rachitsky): Get ahead of it, highlight the good, share the context, have a crystal clear POV on what to do next, and remember: you and your projects are not the same thing.
