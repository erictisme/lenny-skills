# /lenny-product-market-fit - Finding Product-Market Fit

A structured coaching journey through the entire PMF lifecycle — from understanding what PMF actually is, to measuring it, to iterating toward it, to recognizing when you have it — drawing on 25+ founder stories and battle-tested frameworks from Lenny's archive.

## Prerequisites
- Lenny's Data MCP connected (`mcp.lennysdata.com/mcp`)

## About This Journey
You'll learn how to define product-market fit for your specific business, how to measure whether you have it, how to systematically iterate toward it, and what it actually feels like when you get there. Based on 10 articles and podcast episodes featuring firsthand stories from founders of Netflix, Uber, Airbnb, Stripe, Dropbox, Notion, Figma, Superhuman, Vanta, Lattice, Retool, and more — plus Todd Jackson's PMF framework from First Round Capital.

Best for: Early-stage founders (pre-seed through Series A) who have a product or prototype and aren't sure if they've found PMF yet, or B2B/consumer PMs tasked with iterating a product toward stronger fit with their market.

## The Curriculum
1. **What Product-Market Fit Actually Is (and Isn't)** — Understand the real definition of PMF, why it's not binary, and why most founders either can't define it or realize they never fully "felt" it. *(Sources: "How to know if you've got product-market fit," "A guide for finding product-market fit in B2B," Todd Jackson podcast)*
2. **Measuring PMF: The Signals That Matter** — Learn the concrete signals — retention curves, the 40% "very disappointed" test, organic growth, sales yield, burn multiple — and how to use them at your stage. *(Sources: "How to know if you've got product-market fit," "What to ask your users about Product-Market Fit," Todd Jackson podcast)*
3. **The Levels of PMF: Where You Are on the Journey** — Map yourself to Todd Jackson's four levels (nascent, developing, strong, extreme) and understand what to optimize at each stage. *(Sources: Todd Jackson podcast, "A guide for finding product-market fit in B2B")*
4. **Iterating Toward PMF: The Four Ps and How to Pivot** — Learn the Four Ps framework (persona, problem, promise, product) and study how companies like Lattice, Vanta, Plaid, and Ironclad pivoted their way to fit. *(Sources: Todd Jackson podcast, "A guide for finding product-market fit in B2B," Ivan Zhao podcast)*
5. **What PMF Feels Like: Recognizing the Moment** — Hear directly from 25+ founders about the moment they knew — sudden pull, compounding growth, or hitting a milestone — and learn why half of them took 1.5+ years to get there. *(Sources: "What it feels like when you've found product-market fit," "How long it takes to find Product-Market Fit")*
6. **Sustaining and Expanding PMF** — Understand why PMF is never permanent, how to expand fit to new segments, and what to do when growth stalls. *(Sources: "A guide for finding product-market fit in B2B," Todd Jackson podcast, Ivan Zhao podcast)*

## Coaching Session Instructions

When this skill is invoked, follow this exact flow:

### Opening
1. Give a brief, motivating overview: Product-market fit is the single most important thing a startup does in its first few years — and yet most founders have only a vague grasp on what it actually is, how to measure it, or how to systematically work toward it. This journey is going to change that. 3-4 sentences. Make the user feel excited, not overwhelmed.
2. Show the roadmap: "We'll cover 6 concepts. Here's the journey: (1) What PMF actually is, (2) How to measure it, (3) The levels of PMF and where you are, (4) How to iterate toward it using the Four Ps, (5) What it feels like when you find it, (6) How to sustain and expand it. By the end, you'll be able to define what PMF looks like for your specific product, diagnose where you are on the journey, and pick the right levers to move forward."
3. Ask: "Tell me about what you're building: What does your product do, who is it for, and where are you right now — do you have users, are they paying, and what's your honest gut feeling on whether you've found product-market fit or not?"
4. Wait for their answer. Use it to personalize the entire session.

### For Each Concept (walk through in order, one at a time)

#### Concept 1: What Product-Market Fit Actually Is (and Isn't)
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/how-to-know-if-youve-got-product-market-fit.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's collection of PMF definitions, from the most concrete to the least: retention curves flattening (Brian Balfour, Casey Winters), the 40% "very disappointed" survey (Sean Ellis/Rahul Vohra), exponential organic growth (Andy Rachleff), cost-efficient growth / burn multiple (David Sacks), CAC < LTV (Elizabeth Yin), and the visceral feeling of customers clamoring for the product (Marc Andreessen, Steve Blank). Then load `newsletters/a-guide-for-finding-product-market-fit-in-b2b.md` and share the critical insight from Shishir Mehrotra (Coda): "You always have product-market fit with one group and not with the next. The group you haven't achieved it with is the one you really want to target." PMF is not a binary yes/no — it's a spectrum that expands over time.
3. **Apply it:** "Based on what you told me about your product, which of these definitions resonates most? And here's the uncomfortable question: by these definitions, do you think you have PMF today?"
4. **Check understanding:** "In your own words, what is product-market fit for your specific product? Not the generic definition — yours."
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say something generic like "people like it," push back: "Liking isn't enough. As Rick Song from Persona puts it, you don't want to get 'friend-zoned' by your customers — they like you, but they don't need you. What would need to be true for your customers to genuinely need your product?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 1 of 6. Now that we know what PMF is, let's talk about how to actually measure it."

#### Concept 2: Measuring PMF — The Signals That Matter
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/what-to-ask-your-users-about-product-market-fit.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Matt Gallivan's framework for measuring PMF through user research. If you can reach 100+ users, run a survey with three questions: (1) Sean Ellis's "very disappointed" question with the 40% threshold, (2) a comparative utility question — "Compared to other ways you do X, is this product much better, somewhat better, or worse?", (3) a behavioral question — "The last time you did X, what did you use?" These measure affection, relative utility, and actual behavior. If you can't survey 100+ users, talk to 6-12 and ask behavioral questions: "Walk me through the last time you did X." Listen for two signals: "a recognition of explicit, superior value relative to competitors, and genuine enthusiasm." Also share the two questions NOT to ask: NPS (debatable science) and "what do you want us to build?" (people don't know what they want until they see it).
3. **Apply it:** "Which of these measurement approaches can you use right now? Do you have 100+ users for a survey, or are you in the 6-12 conversation zone?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What three questions would you ask your users this week to get a read on your PMF?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they default to NPS, redirect: "Matt Gallivan calls NPS part of the 'NPS Industrial Complex.' The science behind it is debatable. The 'very disappointed' test gives you a much clearer signal. Would you be willing to try that instead?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 2 of 6. You know what PMF is and how to measure it. Next: figuring out exactly where you are on the PMF journey."

#### Concept 3: The Levels of PMF — Where You Are on the Journey
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `podcasts/todd-jackson.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Todd Jackson's four-level PMF framework from First Round Capital. The ultimate goal is "extreme product-market fit: a state of widespread demand for a product that satisfies a critical need and can be delivered repeatably and efficiently." Three dimensions: demand, satisfaction, efficiency — and you trade off between them at each level. Level 1 (Nascent): 3-5 customers, focus on satisfaction, $0-500K ARR, it's okay to be inefficient (Vanta did it manually with spreadsheets). Level 2 (Developing): 5-25 customers, focus on demand, $500K-5M ARR, 10% cold conversion rate. Level 3 (Strong): 25-100+ customers, focus on efficiency, $5M-25M ARR, "the fish are jumping into the boat." Level 4 (Extreme): 100+ customers, $25M+ ARR, all three dimensions humming. The hard truth: roughly 60% of startups never get past Level 2.
3. **Apply it:** "Based on these levels, where do you think you are right now? Level 1, 2, 3, or somewhere in between?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What is the primary dimension you should be optimizing for at your current level — satisfaction, demand, or efficiency — and why?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say efficiency at Level 1, redirect: "Todd Jackson is clear on this — at Level 1, it's okay to be inefficient if it helps you uncover something that delivers insanely good customer satisfaction. Christina Cacioppo at Vanta literally hand-wrote compliance reports pretending to be software. Efficiency comes later."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 3 of 6. You know what PMF is, how to measure it, and where you are. Now the big one: how to actually move forward."

#### Concept 4: Iterating Toward PMF — The Four Ps and How to Pivot
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `podcasts/todd-jackson.md` (if not already loaded, re-read for the Four Ps section)
2. **Teach it:** Share Todd Jackson's Four Ps framework — persona, problem, promise, product. "Your product has to deliver a promise that solves the problem of your persona." When you're stuck, change one or more of the Four Ps. Lattice kept the persona (HR leaders) but changed the problem (from OKRs to performance management) — Jack Altman sold the first 5-10 customers with Figma mockups alone. Vanta changed all four Ps. Plaid kept the product (bank account connections) but flipped everything else — from consumer budgeting to developer API. Then load `podcasts/ivan-zhao.md` and share Ivan Zhao's "sugar-coated broccoli" lesson: Notion spent 2+ years trying to build a developer tool that "everybody can use to create software" — nobody cared. Their breakthrough was hiding the vision inside a productivity tool people already understood. "The world is not like you. The world only cares what's in front of them." As Jack Altman puts it: "Most founders do a 10% pivot, and what they need to be doing is a 200% pivot."
3. **Apply it:** "Let's map your Four Ps right now. Who is your persona? What problem are you solving? What promise are you making? And does your product deliver on that promise? Which of these four feels weakest?"
4. **Check understanding:** "If you had to pivot one of your Four Ps tomorrow, which one would you change and what would you change it to?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they resist pivoting, share Todd Jackson's data: "60% of startups get stuck at Level 1 or Level 2. The ones that break through almost always changed at least one of the Four Ps. Resistance to pivoting is the most common trap."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 4 of 6. Past the halfway mark. Next: what it actually feels like when PMF clicks — stories from 25+ founders."

#### Concept 5: What PMF Feels Like — Recognizing the Moment
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/what-it-feels-like-when-youve-found-product-market-fit.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share the three patterns Lenny found across 25 founder stories. Sign 1 — sudden pull: Drew Houston (Dropbox) described it as "getting pressed into the back of your seat by a fast car." Ryan Graves (Uber) said "zero marketing budget and we were growing like a weed. Word of mouth was uncontrollable." Calvin French-Owen (Segment) said "Before we had PMF, we had to push. When we hit it, we started feeling pull for the first time." Sign 2 — steady compounding: Chris Best (Substack) had "PMF anxiety" that gradually faded as growth compounded until "there wasn't time left in the day to worry about it." Max Mullen (Instacart) said "Initial signs feel like a calm breeze, while true PMF feels like a powerful wind at your back." Sign 3 — hitting a milestone: Joe Gebbia (Airbnb) knew when his mom booked her first Airbnb. Ryan Hoover (Product Hunt) overheard strangers in a coffee shop talking about their upcoming launch and teared up. Then load `newsletters/how-long-it-takes-to-find-product-market-fit.md` and share the timeline data: the average time from launch to PMF is ~1.5 years. Netflix took 18 months, Airbnb took 2 years, Thumbtack took 5 years. Every single marketplace started by concentrating on just one city or category.
3. **Apply it:** "Have you experienced any of these three signs — sudden pull, compounding growth, or a milestone moment? Even a faint version?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What would the 'moment' look like for your product — what specific thing happening would make you say 'We've got something here'?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they describe vanity metrics (press coverage, downloads), redirect: "Be careful of fool's gold. Lenny specifically warns about this — the Yo app and CryptoKitties had massive initial traction that faded fast. The real signal is retention and organic growth that compounds over time, not a spike."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 5 of 6. One more to go: how to keep PMF once you have it — because it's never permanent."

#### Concept 6: Sustaining and Expanding PMF
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/a-guide-for-finding-product-market-fit-in-b2b.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share the critical insight that PMF is never finished. Ali Ghodsi (Databricks) said even at $100M in revenue, "there was always this fear that we were suddenly going to crash into a wall." Tomer London (Gusto): "I never felt at any point like, ah, now I have product-market fit." Jori Lallo (Linear): "PMF has been so gradual; I might even say... linear. It's more like we're chipping away at these obstacles." Rick Song (Persona) frames it as: "Every time you raise, your PMF is gone — you now have to hit the next bar." Share Lenny's five-step B2B PMF ladder: (1) Get one company to love your product, (2) Get one company to pay meaningfully, (3) Get multiple companies to love and pay, (4) Notice the shift from push to pull, (5) Keep growing consistently. Then share Todd Jackson's point about expanding TAM — Vanta went from SOC 2 to trust management platform, Verkada went from cameras to a full security suite. Every great company has to find PMF again and again.
3. **Apply it:** "Where are you on that five-step ladder? And what's the next segment or market you'll need to find fit with after your current one?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why is PMF never permanent, and what's the biggest risk to your current fit?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they think they're "done" with PMF, challenge them: "Shishir Mehrotra ran YouTube at 100 million DAUs and a billion MAUs and still didn't think they had PMF — because they had teenagers but not adults. Casey Winters puts it well: customer expectations ever increase. What segment are you missing?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's all 6 concepts. Let's bring it all together."

## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet
- **PMF as Spectrum, Not Binary** (Shishir Mehrotra / Coda): "You always have product-market fit with one group and not with the next"
- **The 40% "Very Disappointed" Test** (Sean Ellis / Rahul Vohra): Survey users "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" — 40% answering "very disappointed" is the PMF threshold
- **Retention Curve Flattening** (Brian Balfour / Casey Winters): If cohort retention flattens above 0%, some market wants your product; benchmarks vary by category (consumer social ~25%, consumer subscription ~40%, SaaS higher)
- **Burn Multiple** (David Sacks): Net Burn / Net New ARR — low burn multiple means the market is pulling product out of you; high means you're pushing product onto the market
- **Four Levels of PMF** (Todd Jackson / First Round): Nascent (3-5 customers, focus satisfaction), Developing (5-25, focus demand), Strong (25-100+, focus efficiency), Extreme (100+, all three dimensions)
- **Three Dimensions of PMF** (Todd Jackson / First Round): Demand, Satisfaction, Efficiency — you trade off between them at each level, and don't need all three at once early on
- **The Four Ps** (Todd Jackson / First Round): Persona, Problem, Promise, Product — "your product has to deliver a promise that solves the problem of your persona." When stuck, change one or more.
- **The $100 Vending Machine** (Brett Berson / First Round): Demand + satisfaction without efficiency is not real PMF — you need all three eventually
- **Don't Get Friend-Zoned** (Rick Song / Persona): Your customers should need you, not just like you — ask them directly, "If we went away, how painful would that be?"
- **Push vs. Pull** (Multiple founders): Before PMF you're pushing the boulder uphill; after PMF you're chasing it downhill — as Emmett Shear (Twitch) put it, "Both are very demanding, but feel totally different"
- **Sugar-Coated Broccoli** (Ivan Zhao / Notion): Hide your ambitious vision inside a form factor people already understand and care about
- **200% Pivot** (Jack Altman / Lattice): "Most founders do a 10% pivot, and what they need to be doing is a 200% pivot"
- **Three PMF Measurement Questions** (Matt Gallivan): Affection ("very disappointed?"), relative utility ("compared to alternatives?"), behavior ("what did you actually use last time?")
- **Five-Step B2B PMF Ladder** (Lenny Rachitsky): (1) One company loves it, (2) One company pays meaningfully, (3) Multiple companies love and pay, (4) Shift from push to pull, (5) Consistent growth
- **1.5-Year Average to PMF** (Lenny Rachitsky): Median time from launch to feeling PMF across 25+ companies; Netflix 18 months, Airbnb 2 years, Superhuman 3 years, Thumbtack 5 years
- **Median 2 Years in B2B** (Lenny Rachitsky): Median time from idea to PMF feeling across 24 top B2B startups; product-to-PMF typically 9-18 months
