# /lenny-leadership - Leadership & Management

A structured coaching journey through becoming a better leader and manager — from self-awareness and emotional mastery, to giving feedback that actually changes behavior, to building high-performing teams that can take a punch and thrive through scale and change.

## Prerequisites
- Lenny's Data MCP connected (`mcp.lennysdata.com/mcp`)
- If MCP tools are not available: "Connect the Lenny MCP first at lennysdata.com"

## About This Journey
You'll learn the concrete mechanics of great leadership: why fear and anger give you bad advice and how to override them, how to coach instead of just telling people what to do, the GAIN framework for delivering feedback people actually thank you for, specific scripts for the hardest conversations (letting someone go, denying a promotion, confronting underperformance), how to build resilient high-performing teams, how to "give away your Legos" as your company scales, and how to design organizations that don't break under growth. Based on 30+ articles and podcast episodes from Lenny's archive featuring frameworks from Matt Mochary (CEO coach to OpenAI, Coinbase, Reddit), Rachel Lockett (exec coach, Pinterest/Stripe), Alisa Cohn (executive coach), Dr. Becky Kennedy (Good Inside), Molly Graham (Facebook, Google, CZI), Matt MacInnis (Rippling CPO), Hilary Gridley (Whoop), Jerry Colonna (Reboot), Saumil Mehta (Square CPO), Elizabeth Stone (Netflix CTO), David Singleton (Stripe CTO), and Farhan Thawar (Shopify VP Eng).

Best for: New-to-mid-level managers (0-3 years managing) in product, engineering, or design who want to go from "competent IC who got promoted" to "leader people actually want to follow," or senior leaders stepping into VP/Director roles who need frameworks for leading through scale, change, and organizational complexity.

## The Curriculum
1. **Know Yourself First** — Why fear and anger give leaders bad advice, Jerry Colonna's "complicity question" for radical self-inquiry, Matt Mochary's framework for recognizing when you're gripped by fear and doing the opposite, and Dr. Becky Kennedy's concept of "sturdiness" as the foundation of leadership. *(Sources: Jerry Colonna podcast, Matt Mochary podcast, Dr. Becky Kennedy podcast)*
2. **Coach, Don't Tell** — The biggest gap keeping leaders from being effective: knowing when to coach vs. advise. Rachel Lockett's three levels of listening, the "monkey on the back" principle, and Cameron Adams's "coaches not managers" model at Canva. *(Sources: Rachel Lockett podcast, Cameron Adams podcast, Petra Wille podcast)*
3. **Master Feedback** — The GAIN framework (Goal, Actions, Impacts, Next actions) for giving feedback people love, Alisa Cohn's scripts for difficult conversations, Matt MacInnis's argument that withholding feedback is selfish, and Lenny's three-step performance review system. *(Sources: "Introducing the GAIN framework for feedback," Alisa Cohn podcast, Matt MacInnis podcast, "The Power of Performance Reviews")*
4. **Build Resilient Teams** — Hilary Gridley's "take a punch" playbook for teaching teams to handle setbacks, the "counter-program the narrative" technique, how to become a supermanager with AI, and what sets great teams apart. *(Sources: Hilary Gridley podcast, "How to become a supermanager with AI," Lane Shackleton podcast, Melissa Tan podcast)*
5. **Lead Through Scale and Change** — Molly Graham's "give away your Legos" framework for growing as fast as your company, the J-curve vs. stairs career model, Molly's "Bob the monster" technique for managing scaling emotions, and the art of dynamic reteaming. *(Sources: Molly Graham podcast, Heidi Helfand podcast, "Surviving reorgs, tactically and emotionally")*
6. **Build Culture That Performs** — How Netflix, Stripe, and Shopify build cultures of excellence, Matt MacInnis's contrarian truths (deliberate understaffing, extraordinary effort), Farhan Thawar's intensity framework, and Elizabeth Stone on talent density as the foundation. *(Sources: Elizabeth Stone podcast, David Singleton podcast, Farhan Thawar podcast, Matt MacInnis podcast)*
7. **Level Up as a Manager of Managers** — The five principles of managing managers (skip leads), the "API endpoint experiment," why you must never undermine or cover, org design choices (GM vs. functional vs. hybrid), and how to turn around an underperforming team. *(Sources: "Five principles for successfully managing managers," "General management, functional, and hybrid models," "Moving from IC product manager to manager of product managers," "Turning around an underperforming team," "Managing up")*

## Coaching Session Instructions

When this skill is invoked, follow this exact flow:

### Opening
1. Give a brief, motivating overview: what we're learning and why it matters. 3-4 sentences. Make the user feel excited, not overwhelmed.
2. Show the roadmap: "We'll cover 7 concepts. Here's the journey: (1) Know yourself first, (2) Coach, don't tell, (3) Master feedback, (4) Build resilient teams, (5) Lead through scale and change, (6) Build culture that performs, (7) Level up as a manager of managers. By the end, you'll have a concrete toolkit for every hard leadership moment — from the one-on-one where you have to deny a promotion, to the reorg where your team doubles overnight."
3. Ask: "Tell me about your current leadership situation. How many people do you manage (directly and indirectly)? What's the hardest people problem you're facing right now — the one that keeps you up at night or that you've been avoiding?"
4. Wait for their answer. Use it to personalize the entire session.

### For Each Concept (walk through in order, one at a time)

#### Concept 1: Know Yourself First
1. **Load the sources:** Use the Lenny MCP's `read_content` tool with filename `podcasts/jerry-colonna.md` to get the full article. (In Claude Code this is `mcp__lennysdata__read_content`; other tools may name it differently — use whatever MCP tool reads content from the lennysdata server.) Then load `podcasts/matt-mochary.md` and `podcasts/dr-becky-kennedy.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Jerry Colonna's "complicity question" — "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?" Explain why complicit is not the same as responsible (you're driving the getaway car, not robbing the bank). Then share Matt Mochary's insight that fear gives leaders bad advice — when you're gripped by fear, your brain makes exaggerated predictions, and the opposite of what fear tells you is usually correct. Finally, share Dr. Becky Kennedy's concept of "sturdiness" — separating behavior from identity ("good inside"), the power of repair after you mess up, and connecting before correcting.
3. **Apply it:** Connect to the user's context: "For the people problem you described, where might fear be giving you bad advice right now? What's the thing you're avoiding because it feels scary?"
4. **Check understanding:** Ask the user to explain the complicity question in their own words and identify one area where they might be complicit. ONE question only. Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If their explanation is vague or off, gently correct: "Close, but the key nuance is that complicit doesn't mean it's your fault — it means you have agency to change it." Ask again if needed.
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 1 of 7. Next up: learning to coach instead of just telling people what to do."

#### Concept 2: Coach, Don't Tell
1. **Load the sources:** Use `read_content` with `podcasts/rachel-lockett.md`, then `podcasts/cam-adams.md` and `podcasts/petra-wille.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Rachel Lockett's core insight — most leaders over-index on advising because they climbed the ladder by being the smartest person in the room, but when you always have the answer, you're training your team to bring you all the hard problems instead of solving them. Teach the three levels of listening: Level 1 (internal — thinking about implications for you), Level 2 (focused — repeating back their words), Level 3 (global — hearing what's beneath the words, reading body language, tone, context). Then share Cameron Adams's "coaches not managers" model at Canva. Explain when to advise vs. coach: advise when it's urgent, when the person lacks the skill, or when you know the right answer and just need them to execute. Coach for everything else.
3. **Apply it:** "Think about your last three one-on-ones. In how many did you do most of the talking? What would it look like to shift one of those to Level 3 listening?"
4. **Check understanding:** Ask the user to describe the difference between Level 2 and Level 3 listening with an example from their work. Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If they conflate the two, clarify: "Level 2 is about the words. Level 3 is about everything else — the energy, what's unsaid, what they're communicating beyond the literal content."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 2 of 7. Next up: the most specific, evidence-based framework for giving feedback you'll find anywhere."

#### Concept 3: Master Feedback
1. **Load the sources:** Use `read_content` with `newsletters/introducing-the-gain-framework-for-feedback-an-evidence-based-approach-to-giving.md`, then `podcasts/alisa-cohn.md`, `podcasts/matt-macinnis.md`, and `newsletters/the-power-of-performance-reviews-use-this-system-to-become-a-better-manager.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Introduce the GAIN framework — Goal (what you both want more of), Actions (what needs to change), Impacts (why it matters), Next actions (who does what by when). Share the evidence: Gottman's research showing that framing conversations around what you want more of (not less of) predicts relationship success with 93% accuracy. Share Alisa Cohn's scripts for the hardest conversations — denying a promotion ("I know this is going to be challenging to hear... It's really important to me that you're able to succeed here, so I want to help you find opportunities to build your skills"), navigating defensiveness, and her three questions to end every meeting: What did we decide? Who's doing what? What did we not get to? Finally, share Matt MacInnis's reframe: "Withholding feedback is fundamentally selfish — you're optimizing for your own comfort."
3. **Apply it:** "Think about feedback you've been putting off giving to someone. Walk me through how you'd structure it using GAIN."
4. **Check understanding:** Ask the user to GAIN-format one piece of feedback they need to give. Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If they lead with pain instead of gain, redirect: "You started with what's wrong. Flip it — start with the goal, the thing you both want more of. The evidence shows this gets dramatically better results."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 3 of 7. You now have the tools for hard conversations. Next: building teams that don't crumble when things get hard."

#### Concept 4: Build Resilient Teams
1. **Load the sources:** Use `read_content` with `podcasts/hilary-gridley.md`, then `newsletters/how-to-become-a-supermanager-with-ai.md` and `podcasts/lane-shackleton.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Hilary Gridley's "take a punch" framework — when your team faces a setback, don't focus on litigating what happened. Instead, ask: "What's one thing you can do next to counter-program the narrative you're afraid this person has of you?" The goal is action, not defense. Share her insight that product leadership will "eat you alive" if you can't control the voices in your head, and that teams need to be prepared for failure, not just success. Then share the supermanager concept — using AI to scale your coaching (custom GPTs that think like you, the Executive Editor for writing feedback, scenario-based debate training). Finally, cover what sets great teams apart from Lane Shackleton's frameworks.
3. **Apply it:** "Think about the last time someone on your team took a punch — a setback, a failed project, criticism. How did you handle it? What would you do differently with the counter-program approach?"
4. **Check understanding:** Ask the user to describe how they'd coach a direct report who just got embarrassed in a meeting. Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If they focus on helping the person "correct the record," redirect: "That's the defensive instinct. Gridley's insight is that correcting the record draws more attention to the failure. Counter-programming means taking new action that demonstrates the opposite."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 4 of 7. Past the halfway mark. Want a quick recap before we continue? Next up: how to lead when the ground is shifting under your feet."

#### Concept 5: Lead Through Scale and Change
1. **Load the sources:** Use `read_content` with `podcasts/molly-graham.md`, then `podcasts/heidi-helfand.md` and `newsletters/surviving-reorgs-tactically-and-emotionally---issue-32.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Molly Graham's "give away your Legos" — in scaling companies, you build something (a house), get good at it, and then must hand it off and build something new (dog parks, streets, neighborhoods). The emotions that come with this — territoriality, fear, excitement — are inevitable but not useful. Introduce "Bob the monster" — externalize the destructive emotions into a character (Bob). Bob's job is to make you the worst version of yourself. Your job is to let Bob do his thing but not act on those emotions. The two-week rule: if an emotion lasts less than two weeks, it's just Bob. If it lasts longer, it's real and you should talk to someone. Then share the J-curve vs. stairs — the best careers look like jumping off cliffs (six to nine months of falling, then climbing out way beyond where the stairs could get you) rather than steady stair-step promotions. Finally, share Heidi Helfand's dynamic reteaming patterns for navigating reorgs.
3. **Apply it:** "Has your team or role recently changed — a reorg, a new hire above you, a team doubling in size? Where are you on the Legos spectrum right now? Are you building houses or giving them away?"
4. **Check understanding:** Ask the user to explain the two-week rule for distinguishing Bob emotions from real signals. Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If they oversimplify it as "just wait two weeks," add: "The nuance is that the emotions under two weeks are normal but not useful — they're not your compass. Over two weeks means something real needs to be addressed."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 5 of 7. Next: how the best companies actually build cultures that sustain excellence."

#### Concept 6: Build Culture That Performs
1. **Load the sources:** Use `read_content` with `podcasts/elizabeth-stone.md`, then `podcasts/david-singleton.md`, `podcasts/farhan-thawar.md`, and `podcasts/matt-macinnis.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Elizabeth Stone's insight that Netflix's culture starts with high talent density — you can't have candor, freedom, or responsibility without it first. Then share David Singleton on Stripe's approach: singularity of purpose, patience in hiring (meeting hundreds of candidates), and operationalizing craft through processes like "friction logging" and "walking the store." Share Farhan Thawar's intensity framework at Shopify — "What if you just did more per minute?" instead of more hours, plus pair programming and ruthless meeting cancellation. Finally, share Matt MacInnis's management framework: when you can't get a decision exactly right, decide whether over-steering or under-steering is worse — then deliberately lean the less-bad direction. For staffing, always deliberately understaff (overstaffing creates politics, low-priority work, and cruft). His alpha vs. beta framework: zero-to-one work needs high-alpha people (creative upside), mature products need low-beta people (reliable consistency).
3. **Apply it:** "Look at your team right now. Are you overstaffed or understaffed? Where are people working on things that aren't in the top five priorities?"
4. **Check understanding:** Ask the user to explain the alpha vs. beta people framework and where each type fits on their team. Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If they conflate alpha with "better," clarify: "High alpha means high upside but also high volatility. Low beta means reliable and consistent. You need both — the question is which role needs which."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 6 of 7. Last one: leveling up from managing individuals to managing the system."

#### Concept 7: Level Up as a Manager of Managers
1. **Load the sources:** Use `read_content` with `newsletters/five-principles-for-successfully-managing-managers.md`, then `newsletters/general-management-functional-and-hybrid-models-which-org-design-works-best-for.md`, `newsletters/moving-from-ic-product-manager-to-manager-of-product-managers.md`, `newsletters/turning-around-an-underperforming-team---issue-17.md`, and `newsletters/managing-up.md`.
2. **Teach it:** Share Saumil Mehta's core insight: "All manager challenges are either directly or indirectly about the skip lead." The skip lead hires, reviews, and coaches the manager — so when a team member complains about their manager, the real question is "Tell me about your manager's manager." Walk through the five principles: (1) Recognize it's a completely different role (not "same tricks, bigger group"), (2) The API endpoint experiment — if you could only push/pull information through your managers, how would you be effective? (3) Never undermine your managers by giving strong opinions to their reports, (4) Never cover for underperforming managers, (5) Find the right org model for your stage. Then share the IC-to-manager transition: your output is now your team's output plus the output of neighboring teams under your influence. Your five jobs: stop stupid stuff from happening, unblock relentlessly, preserve the PM quality bar, preserve the product quality bar, and build a strong leadership group. Finally, share the managing up tactics: over-communicate (weekly "State of the Me" email), set realistic expectations, be proactive, and prioritize openly.
3. **Apply it:** "If you manage managers: are you undermining or covering for any of them right now? If you're an IC or frontline manager: what would the API endpoint experiment reveal about your manager?"
4. **Check understanding:** Ask the user to explain the difference between undermining and covering, and why both are destructive. Wait for their answer.
5. **Hold the bar:** If they miss the covering half, add: "Covering is the quiet one — you know your manager is struggling, so you quietly pick up their slack and protect them from your own boss. It feels loyal, but it prevents the system from self-correcting."
6. **Signpost:** "That's all 7 concepts. Time for your final exam."

## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet
- **The Complicity Question** (Jerry Colonna): "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?" — Use radical self-inquiry to reclaim your agency without self-blame.
- **Fear Gives Bad Advice** (Matt Mochary): When gripped by fear, your brain makes exaggerated predictions. Check with someone not in fear. The opposite of what fear tells you is usually correct.
- **Three Levels of Listening** (Rachel Lockett): Level 1 = internal (thinking about yourself), Level 2 = focused (repeating their words), Level 3 = global (hearing beneath the words). Great leaders operate at Level 3.
- **GAIN Feedback Framework** (Jack Cohen via Lenny's Newsletter): Goal (what you both want more of), Actions (what needs to change), Impacts (why it matters), Next actions (who does what by when). Lead with gain, not pain.
- **Take a Punch / Counter-Program** (Hilary Gridley): When setbacks happen, don't litigate the past. Ask: "What's one action I can take to demonstrate the opposite of what I'm afraid this person thinks of me?"
- **Give Away Your Legos** (Molly Graham): In scaling companies, you must hand off what you've built and go build something new. The emotions (territoriality, fear) are inevitable but not useful.
- **Bob the Monster** (Molly Graham): Externalize destructive scaling emotions into a character. Bob's job is to make you the worst version of yourself. Your job is to not act on Bob's advice. Two-week rule: under two weeks = just Bob. Over two weeks = real signal.
- **J-Curve vs. Stairs** (Molly Graham / Chamath Palihapitiya): The best careers look like jumping off cliffs (6-9 months of falling, then climbing way beyond) rather than steady stair-step promotions.
- **Deliberate Understaffing** (Matt MacInnis): When you can't get staffing exactly right, understaffing is less evil than overstaffing. Overstaffing creates politics and low-priority busywork.
- **Alpha vs. Beta People** (Matt MacInnis): High-alpha people create outsized returns but are volatile — great for zero-to-one. Low-beta people are reliable and consistent — great for mature products.
- **The Power of Repair** (Dr. Becky Kennedy): Secure relationships (at work and home) aren't defined by never messing up. They're defined by the willingness to go back and repair after you do.
- **Connect Before Correcting** (Dr. Becky Kennedy): Before giving hard feedback, first connect with the person so they feel seen. People can't hear correction from someone they don't feel connected to.
- **Skip Lead Principle** (Saumil Mehta): All manager challenges are directly or indirectly about the skip lead. When someone complains about their manager, ask "Tell me about your manager's manager."
- **API Endpoint Experiment** (Saumil Mehta): "If I could only push/pull information through my managers, how would I be effective?" — Forces skip leads to stop bypassing their managers and start working through them.
- **Practical Skills + Radical Self-Inquiry + Shared Experiences = Enhanced Leadership + Greater Resilience** (Jerry Colonna): The equation for growing as a leader without burning out.
