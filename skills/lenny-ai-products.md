# /lenny-ai-products - Building AI Products

A structured coaching journey through building great AI products — from shifting your mindset away from traditional software, to mastering evals, designing the right development lifecycle, leveraging AI agents, and building an AI-native organization.

## About This Journey
You'll learn the complete playbook for building AI products that actually work — not just impressive demos. Starting with why AI products require fundamentally different thinking, you'll work through the counterintuitive lessons from 20+ top builders, the eval systems that separate great AI products from failed ones, the CC/CD development lifecycle built for non-deterministic systems, how to use AI agents in your own product work, and how AI-native companies like Every are operating with 15 people and multiple products. Based on 20+ articles and podcast episodes from Lenny's archive featuring builders from Anthropic, OpenAI, Intercom, GitHub, Superhuman, Adobe, Shopify, Duolingo, and more.

Best for: PMs or founders who are shipping (or about to ship) their first AI-powered feature or product and need a practical framework — not abstract AI strategy — for how to build, evaluate, and iterate on non-deterministic systems.

## Coaching Session Instructions

When this skill is invoked, follow this exact flow:

### Opening
1. Give a brief, motivating overview: what we're learning and why it matters. 3-4 sentences. Make the user feel excited, not overwhelmed.
2. Show the roadmap: "We'll cover 6 concepts. Here's the journey: (1) Think AI-native not AI-bolt-on, (2) The AI development lifecycle is different, (3) Evals — the make-or-break skill, (4) From agents to workflows, (5) The AI-native organization, (6) Putting it all together. ### For Each Concept (walk through in order, one at a time)

#### Concept 1: Think AI-Native, Not AI-Bolt-On
1. **Load the source:** Use the Lenny MCP's `read_content` tool with filename `newsletters/counterintuitive-advice-for-building-ai-products.md` to get the full article. (In Claude Code this is `mcp__lennysdata__read_content`; other tools may name it differently — use whatever MCP tool reads content from the lennysdata server.)
2. **Teach it:** Share the core insight from Lenny and Kyle Poyar's interviews with 20+ top AI builders: most early AI products fail because teams bolt AI onto existing workflows instead of rethinking the experience. As Elad Gil puts it, "The first-pass product is often a bolt-on or simple chat experience. The high-value experience is a deeper rethink." Paul Adams from Intercom describes the fundamental mindset shift: "With AI, it is wholly unclear if something is possible to build. The best place to start is by asking, 'What is technologically possible?' and prototyping." This flips the traditional product process on its head — you used to start with the user problem, now you start with what's technically possible. Then share three critical lessons: (1) "Demo value isn't user value" — Joshua Xu of HeyGen warns that a cool demo doesn't mean you have a product customers love. (2) Scott Belsky argues "the data and the interfaces may become more important than the models themselves" — companies with proprietary data in a deep vertical will have the advantage. (3) Rahul Vohra of Superhuman learned that "speed wins" — pre-computing AI responses so they're instant is a massive UX lever. Then load `podcasts/mike-krieger.md` and share Mike Krieger's observation from Anthropic: the biggest bottleneck is no longer engineering — it's decision-making and alignment upstream, and the merge queue downstream. When 90% of your code is AI-written, the constraints shift.
3. **Apply it:** "Look at what you're building — is it a bolt-on (adding an AI chat sidebar to an existing product) or a rethink (reimagining the workflow entirely)? Where does your product sit on that spectrum?"
4. **Check understanding:** "In your own words, what's the difference between bolting AI onto a product versus building AI-native? And why does Paul Adams say AI flips the traditional product process?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "we're adding a chatbot to our dashboard," push back: "That's the bolt-on pattern. As Elad Gil says, that's the first pass. The high-value experience comes from a deeper rethink. What workflow could you reimagine entirely if the AI could do 80% of it autonomously? Start from what's technically possible, not from your current UI."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 1 of 6. You now have the mindset shift. Next: the specific development lifecycle that AI products need — because they break the assumptions of traditional software."

#### Concept 2: The AI Development Lifecycle Is Different
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/why-your-ai-product-needs-a-different-development-lifecycle.md`
2. **Teach it:** Walk through Aishwarya Reganti and Kiriti Badam's CC/CD framework — Continuous Calibration / Continuous Development — built from leading 50+ AI implementations at OpenAI, Google, Amazon, and Databricks. AI products are different for two reasons: (1) They're inherently non-deterministic — "the same request can produce different results depending on phrasing, context, or even a different model." (2) Every AI product negotiates a tradeoff between agency and control — "every time you give an AI system more agency, you give up some control." The CC/CD framework works in a continuous loop: Continuous Development (scope capability, set up application, design evals) → Deploy → Continuous Calibration (run evals, analyze behavior and spot error patterns, apply fixes). The critical insight is to version your product by agency level, not feature depth. v1 should be high-control/low-agency (e.g., routing tickets), v2 adds some autonomy (e.g., suggesting responses for human review), v3 earns full agency (e.g., auto-resolving). As they put it: "We often compare working with AI to onboarding a new teammate. You don't hand them your highest-stakes projects on day one."
3. **Apply it:** "For your AI product, what would v1, v2, and v3 look like on the agency-control spectrum? What's the lowest-agency, highest-control version you could ship first?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What are the two reasons AI products break traditional software assumptions? And why do Aish and Kiriti say you should version by agency level, not by feature set?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they want to jump straight to full autonomy, push back: "That's the most common mistake they see — jumping to full agency before testing under high control. If your system suggests a response, you can override it. If it sends the response automatically, you'd better be sure it's right. Start with v1 where a human is always in the loop."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 2 of 6. You now have the development lifecycle. Next: the single skill that both the CPO of Anthropic and the CPO of OpenAI say is the most important for AI product builders — evals."

#### Concept 3: Evals — The Make-or-Break Skill
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/beyond-vibe-checks-a-pms-complete-guide-to-evals.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Aman Khan's core argument: "Every PM building with generative AI obsesses over crafting better prompts, yet almost no one masters the hidden lever behind every exceptional AI product: evaluations. Prompts may make headlines, but evals quietly decide whether your product thrives or dies." Explain what evals are using his analogy: traditional software testing is like checking if a train stays on its tracks — deterministic, clear pass/fail. Evals for AI are like driving a car through a busy city — the environment is variable, the system is non-deterministic. Walk through the four-part eval formula: (1) Setting the role for the judge-LLM, (2) Providing the context (the data to grade), (3) Providing the goal (what does "good" and "bad" look like?), (4) Defining the terminology and label. Then load `newsletters/building-eval-systems-that-improve-your-ai-product.md` and share Hamel Husain and Shreya Shankar's three-phase playbook: Phase 1 is error analysis — start with a "benevolent dictator" (one domain expert) doing open coding on ~100 user interactions, writing free-form critiques, then grouping into failure categories via axial coding. Phase 2 is building the eval suite — use code-based evaluators for objective failures and LLM-as-a-judge for subjective ones, validating with train/dev/test splits and measuring TPR/TNR, not accuracy. Phase 3 is operationalizing — CI golden datasets catch regressions, production monitoring catches unknown unknowns, creating an improvement flywheel.
3. **Apply it:** "For your AI product, what are the top 3 ways it could fail that would make users lose trust? Those are your first eval candidates."
4. **Check understanding:** "What's the difference between a vibe check and a proper eval? And why do Hamel and Shreya insist on a 'benevolent dictator' rather than a committee for error analysis?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "we'll just use off-the-shelf hallucination scores," correct: "Hamel explicitly warns against this. Off-the-shelf metrics like hallucination and toxicity often don't correlate with the actual problems your users face. You can't know what to measure until you systematically find out how your product fails in specific contexts. Start with error analysis — look at 100 real interactions — then build custom evals for the failure patterns you discover."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 3 of 6. You now have the eval playbook. We're at the halfway mark. Next: how to use AI agents to actually do your product work better — and what that reveals about building AI products."

#### Concept 4: From Agents to Workflows
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/make-product-management-fun-again-with-ai-agents.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Tal Raviv's practical framework for AI agents. First, his definition: "agent" is a spectrum — AI systems become agentic the more they (1) act proactively, (2) make a plan, (3) leverage context, (4) draw on live data, (5) take real-world action, and (6) create their own feedback loop. For product builders, the key is his five-step planning checklist: (1) Do I understand this task? — "Understanding a problem should be the only prerequisite to solving it." (2) Could I start even smaller? — If your dream is to monitor five competitor websites, launch with one. (3) Can I keep the downside low? — Instead of posting in a channel, send yourself a DM. Instead of making a decision, make a recommendation. (4) Am I giving enough context? — Share your prioritization framework, your team directory. (5) Am I staying close to raw customer signals? — "Product managers: AI isn't endangering your job, but letting AI read for you is." Use AI to traverse, cluster, and clean up data, but insist on exact quotes and direct links to original sources. Then load `newsletters/build-your-personal-ai-copilot.md` and share Tal Raviv's AI copilot concept: when you give an LLM ongoing context about your goals, role, projects, team, and org, it becomes a real thinking partner. The key is that "AI tools felt like blunt, generic instruments because I wasn't providing enough context."
3. **Apply it:** "Think about the most repetitive, energy-draining part of building your AI product — user research synthesis, writing specs, monitoring production quality. Could you delegate just that one task to an agent? What would the lowest-downside version look like?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What are Tal's five questions you should ask before building an agent? And why does he warn against letting AI summarize everything for you?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they want to build a complex multi-step agent right away, push back: "Tal's biggest lesson from running workshops with 5,000+ PMs is that people over-scope their first agent. Start with one data source, one trigger, one action. AI agents aren't magical genies — they're junior interns. You need to be able to explain the task clearly before you can delegate it."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 4 of 6. You now know how to use agents in your own work. Next: how the most AI-native companies in the world are structured — and what you can steal from them."

#### Concept 5: The AI-Native Organization
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `podcasts/dan-shipper.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Dan Shipper's insights from building Every — a company at the "very bleeding edge" of AI-native operations. Every has 15 people, runs 5 products, publishes a daily newsletter, and does consulting — all with AI. Key organizational moves: (1) Hire a Head of AI Operations — someone who sits with the CEO weekly, identifies repetitive tasks, and builds prompts and workflows so everyone automates without having to learn themselves. As Dan puts it: "Having an AI operations lead lets you identify those things and have them solved without people who are doing the work actually having to take time to do it." (2) Practice "compounding engineering" — "for every unit of work, you should make the next unit of work easier to do." Instead of writing PRDs from scratch each time, write a prompt that turns rambling thoughts into PRDs. (3) Don't repeat yourself — codify your taste, feedback patterns, and decision criteria into prompts that push your judgment to the edges. Dan codified editorial feedback into prompts so writers talk to "a simulation of a simulation of me" before he ever sees their work. Then load `newsletters/25-proven-tactics-to-accelerate-ai-adoption-at-your-company.md` and share the five patterns from AI-forward companies: Shopify includes specific AI tactics in performance reviews, Zapier declared a "code red moment" and gave everyone a week to experiment, Duolingo gave every employee $300 to try AI tools, Intercom's CTO spent a week embedded with individual teams to find 2x opportunities, and Ramp built AI personas loaded with user research that PMs can test any spec against.
3. **Apply it:** "Of Dan's three moves — AI operations lead, compounding engineering, codifying your taste into prompts — which one could you implement this week, even in a small way? And which of the adoption tactics from Shopify/Duolingo/Ramp resonates most with your team's situation?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What does Dan mean by 'compounding engineering'? And why does he say the biggest predictor of whether a company will succeed with AI is not the technology they use?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they think AI adoption is just about buying the right tools, push back: "Peter Yang's research found that the biggest barrier to AI adoption isn't technology — it's organizational change. Employees struggle with vague AI mandates and procurement bottlenecks. The companies that succeed provide specific tactics that employees and teams can adopt. As Shopify's Tobi Lütke showed, saying 'we are AI-first' means nothing if employees don't know what that means for their day-to-day work."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 5 of 6. Almost there. The final concept ties everything together — how AI is changing the act of building software itself."

#### Concept 6: Putting It All Together — Ship, Eval, Iterate
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `podcasts/mike-krieger.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Mike Krieger's observations from Anthropic, where he's "patient zero" for AI-native product development. At Anthropic, 90% of code is now AI-written. Over half of pull requests are Claude Code generated — "probably over 70% at this point." The Claude Code team uses Claude Code to build Claude Code — "a very self-improving kind of way." This changes three things: (1) Bottlenecks shift — "We really rapidly became bottlenecked on other things like our merge queue" and on upstream decision-making and alignment. Mike's question now: "How do I provide the minimum viable strategy to let people feel empowered to go run and type and build?" (2) Reviews change — the Claude Code team realized "Claude is generally right and it's producing pull requests that are probably larger than most people are going to be able to review. So can you use a different Claude to review it?" (3) Prototyping happens earlier — "PMs and designers that have an idea will use Claude to put together an actual functional demo." Then load `newsletters/everyone-should-be-using-claude-code-more.md` and share Lenny's key reframe: "Forget that it's called Claude Code and instead think of it as Claude Local or Claude Agent." It's not about coding — it's a super-intelligent AI running locally that can process your files, organize your work, and do your bidding. Dan Shipper calls it "the most underrated tool for non-technical people." The 50 examples in the article — from synthesizing customer call transcripts to building presentations to roadmapping — show that the line between "building products" and "using products" is dissolving.
3. **Apply it:** "Given everything we've covered — AI-native thinking, the CC/CD lifecycle, evals, agents, organizational change — what's the single most impactful change you could make to how you're building your AI product right now?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Where do the bottlenecks shift when AI writes most of your code? And why does Mike Krieger say the process of knowing what to ask the AI is still a 'very difficult and specialized skill'?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they think AI-written code means engineers become unnecessary, correct: "Mike is explicit about this — 'the role of engineering has changed a lot, but the suite of people that come together to produce a product hasn't yet.' ## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet
- **AI-Native vs. AI-Bolt-On** (Elad Gil): The first-pass product is often a bolt-on or simple chat. The high-value experience requires a deeper rethink of what's technically possible.
- **CC/CD — Continuous Calibration / Continuous Development** (Aishwarya Reganti & Kiriti Badam): AI products need a different lifecycle. Version by agency level (low → high), not feature depth. Development loop: scope → setup → design evals → deploy → run evals → analyze errors → apply fixes.
- **The Agency-Control Tradeoff** (Reganti & Badam): Every time you give an AI system more agency, you give up control. Agency must be earned, not granted.
- **The Eval Formula** (Aman Khan): Every great eval has four parts: setting the role, providing the context, providing the goal, and defining the terminology and label.
- **Three-Phase Eval Playbook** (Hamel Husain & Shreya Shankar): Phase 1: Error analysis with a "benevolent dictator" doing open coding → axial coding. Phase 2: Build eval suite (code-based for objective, LLM-as-judge for subjective). Phase 3: Operationalize with CI golden datasets + production monitoring flywheel.
- **The Agent Spectrum** (Tal Raviv): AI becomes "agentic" as it gains: proactive action, planning, context, live data, real-world action, self-feedback. Start small, keep downside low, stay close to raw customer signals.
- **Compounding Engineering** (Dan Shipper / Every): For every unit of work, make the next unit easier. Build prompts that turn rambling into PRDs. Codify taste into reusable systems.
- **The "Leash" Definition of AGI** (Dan Shipper): AGI = when it's economically profitable to run agents indefinitely. The leash keeps getting longer — from tab-complete to 20-minute autonomous sessions.
- **AI Operations Lead** (Dan Shipper / Every): A dedicated person who identifies repetitive tasks across the org and builds prompts/workflows to automate them — without requiring the workers themselves to learn.
- **"Demo Value Isn't User Value"** (Joshua Xu / HeyGen): A cool AI demo doesn't mean you have a product customers love. Retention and real-world use are what matter.
- **Proprietary Data Advantage** (Scott Belsky / Adobe): Models are commoditizing. Companies with proprietary or uniquely structured data — and a license for it — will win. Designers become more important than ever.
- **Speed Wins** (Rahul Vohra / Superhuman): Pre-computing AI responses so they're instant — rather than generating on demand — is a massive lever on user experience.
- **Minimum Viable Strategy** (Mike Krieger / Anthropic): When AI writes 90% of code, the bottleneck shifts to decision-making and alignment. Provide "the minimum viable strategy to let people feel empowered to go run and build."
