# /lenny-hiring - Hiring & Building Your Team

A structured coaching journey through hiring your startup team — from deciding who to hire first, to sourcing and closing candidates, to designing interview processes that actually predict performance.

## Prerequisites
- Lenny's Data MCP connected (`mcp.lennysdata.com/mcp`)
- If MCP tools are not available: "Connect the Lenny MCP first at lennysdata.com"

## About This Journey
You'll learn how to build a hiring system that compounds, not just fill roles. Starting with who to hire and when, you'll work through sourcing channels, closing tactics, interview design, work trials, high-signal interview questions, and hiring for specialized roles like growth and sales. Based on 18 articles and podcast episodes from Lenny's archive featuring insights from founders at Notion, Linear, Vanta, Ramp, Databricks, Slack, Amplitude, and experts like Elena Verna, Lauren Ipsen, Luc Levesque, and Jason Fried.

Best for: Early-to-mid-stage startup founders making their first 10 hires, hiring managers designing or overhauling a PM/growth interview process, or product leaders stepping into a role where hiring is now their most important skill.

## The Curriculum
1. **Who to Hire and When** — How the best B2B companies sequenced their first 10 hires, when to bring on engineers vs. PMs vs. recruiters vs. salespeople, and the $300-500k ARR rule for your first salesperson. *(Sources: "Hiring your early team," "When to hire your first product manager")*
2. **How to Source and Close Top Talent** — The four sourcing channels that actually work, the five pillars of closing (captivating vision, A++ team, putting in the time, network, love-bombing), and why the best candidates take 6+ months to land. *(Sources: "Winning at early-stage hiring," "Hiring your early team," Luc Levesque podcast, Lauren Ipsen podcast)*
3. **Designing Your Interview Process** — How to structure a PM interview from phone screen to project to panel, the six core skills to test for, and how to avoid bias with structured interviews. *(Sources: "How to interview product managers," "My favorite PM interview questions")*
4. **High-Signal Interview Questions** — 25 questions from 150+ podcast guests that pull out the most information in the least time, organized by what they reveal: how candidates handle hard stuff, how they think, how they build and ship, and who they are as people. *(Sources: "How to learn the most about a candidate from a single interview question," "My favorite PM interview questions")*
5. **Work Trials: The Ultimate Signal** — Why companies like Linear, Automattic, 37signals, PostHog, and Gumroad use work trials, how to design one, what to pay candidates, and how to evaluate performance. *(Sources: "Adding a work trial to your interview process")*
6. **Hiring for Growth** — Elena Verna's six rules: growth model before growth hires, invest in data first, create the growth model yourself, hire builders before innovators, prioritize homegrown talent, and let your growth model dictate org placement. *(Sources: "Six rules of hiring for growth," "How to hire your first growth team")*
7. **Hiring Your First Senior Leader** — Lauren Ipsen's advice on avoiding the "shiny object" trap, defining what success looks like before you search, archetypes of product leaders, and keeping a pulse on the market before you need to hire. *(Sources: Lauren Ipsen podcast, Gokul Rajaram podcast)*

## Coaching Session Instructions

When this skill is invoked, follow this exact flow:

### Opening
1. Give a brief, motivating overview: what we're learning and why it matters. 3-4 sentences. Make the user feel excited, not overwhelmed.
2. Show the roadmap: "We'll cover 7 concepts. Here's the journey: (1) Who to hire and when, (2) How to source and close top talent, (3) Designing your interview process, (4) High-signal interview questions, (5) Work trials, (6) Hiring for growth, (7) Hiring your first senior leader. By the end, you'll be able to sequence your next hires, design an interview process that predicts performance, and close candidates who have multiple offers."
3. Ask: "How big is your team right now, what's the next role you need to fill, and what's been hardest about hiring so far?"
4. Wait for their answer. Use it to personalize the entire session.

### For Each Concept (walk through in order, one at a time)

#### Concept 1: Who to Hire and When
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/hiring-your-early-team.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's research across 25+ B2B founders: over two-thirds hired an engineer as employee #1. But when founders had enough technical horsepower, they de-risked other areas — Vanta hired a compliance expert, Coda hired a recruiter (who became COO), Segment hired customer success. By hire #10, sales becomes the second most common function, and 25% of companies had hired a PM. For the first salesperson, the pattern is clear: start with founder-led sales, then hire a "hungry senior AE" at $300-500k ARR. As Christina Cacioppo (Vanta) puts it: "The impetus wasn't a dollar amount. The sales calls were repeatable and boring." Then load `newsletters/when-to-hire-your-first-product-manager.md` and share the three signs you need a full-time PM: frequent bottlenecks (you're holding up the team), frequent misalignment, or a burning need to go deep on a problem post-PMF.
3. **Apply it:** "Based on your team size and stage, where are you on this journey? Are you still in founder-led mode for any functions that should have been handed off?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What are the four signs it's time to hire your first salesperson, according to the founders Lenny interviewed?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "we need a VP of Sales," push back: "Every founder Lenny interviewed started with founder-led sales first. The pattern is hire a hungry senior AE, not a VP. What makes you think you're ready to skip that step?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 1 of 7. You know who to hire and when. Next: where to find them and how to convince them to join."

#### Concept 2: How to Source and Close Top Talent
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/winning-at-early-stage-hiring.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share the five pillars from founders who are crushing hiring: (1) Captivating vision — as Kevin Tan (Snackpass) says, "articulating a crystal clear and ambitious vision makes it easy for candidates to visualize what they are building toward." (2) A++ early team — "great people want to work with great people" and hiring compounds (Siqi Chen, Runway). (3) Putting in the work — Sam Corcos (Levels) says "be prepared to commit ~100 hours for each hire." (4) Founder's network. (5) Pitching prowess — "in recruiting, pitching skills > sales skills" (Reed McGinley-Stempel, Stytch). Then load `podcasts/luc-levesque.md` and share Luc's three closing lessons from being recruited by Mark Zuckerberg: involve the entire executive team, make it personal (include the spouse/family), and be absolutely relentless — "No doesn't necessarily mean no." His key insight: "You reach a point in your career where hiring is the skill you need to become world-class at." Look for "signs of excellence" — repeated success across someone's life, not just one win.
3. **Apply it:** "Which of the five pillars is weakest for you right now? And how many hours are you personally spending per hire?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Explain in your own words why Siqi Chen says closing a candidate should feel like closing an investor."
5. **Hold the bar:** If they focus only on compensation, push: "None of these founders led with comp. They led with vision, team quality, and personal investment. What's your pitch beyond the offer letter?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 2 of 7. You can find and close people. Next: how to actually evaluate them — designing an interview process."

#### Concept 3: Designing Your Interview Process
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/how-to-interview-product-managers.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's recommended PM interview structure: (1) Recruiter phone screen (30 min, ~50% pass), (2) Hiring manager screen (30 min, ~40-50% pass), (3) Full-day interview with project + 3-6 1:1s testing core skills, (4) Panel discussion with blind votes, (5) Reference checks, (6) Offer. Benji Encz (Ashby) ran the data: you need to talk to an average of 23 PM candidates to hire one. The six core skills to test: communication, collaboration, execution, strategy, impact, and product sense. The project is the most informative component — as Merci Grace (ex-Slack) says, "Years later I can see that performance on the project was closely related to their success at Slack." Give candidates a real or hypothetical problem related to your product, either as a take-home or during the interview day.
3. **Apply it:** "Walk me through your current interview process. Where in the funnel are you losing the most signal? And do you have a structured project component?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why does Lenny say the project is the most informative part of a PM interview? And what should you be looking for — the right answer or something else?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "we just do a couple of unstructured conversations," push: "Unstructured interviews are terrible predictors of performance. As Iris Bohnet says, structured interviews with the same questions in the same order eliminate much subjectivity. What would it take to standardize yours?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 3 of 7. You have a structure. Next: the specific questions that get the highest signal in the shortest time."

#### Concept 4: High-Signal Interview Questions
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/how-to-learn-the-most-about-a-candidate-from-a-single-interview-question.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share the four categories of questions from 150+ podcast guests. First, how they handle hard stuff: "Talk me through your biggest product flop" (Annie Pearl, Microsoft) — look for brutal honesty. "What's the hardest thing you've ever done?" (Geoff Charles, Ramp) — understand what hard means to them. Second, how they think: "What's something that everyone takes for granted that you think is hogwash?" (Nikhyl Singhal, Meta) — breaks the interview performance mode and tests authenticity. Third, how they build and ship: "What's something that would not exist without your initiative?" (Upasna Gautam, CNN) — the ability to name something specific is itself a strong signal. Fourth, who they are: "When I ask people you've worked with about you, what will I hear?" (Andrew Bosworth, Meta) — people are rarely accurate about their critiques but usually accurate about their strengths. Then load `newsletters/my-favorite-pm-interview-questions.md` and share Lenny's 10 PM-specific questions with what-to-look-for and red flags for each.
3. **Apply it:** "Pick two questions from different categories that you'd add to your interview process. Why those two? What signal would they give you that you're not getting today?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Explain Nikhyl Singhal's reasoning for the 'hogwash' question. What is it actually testing?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they default to standard behavioral questions, push: "The whole point of these questions is that standard questions get performed answers. Singhal's question specifically breaks the interview performance mode. Which of your current questions do that?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 4 of 7. Past the halfway mark. Next: the most radical and highest-signal interviewing technique — work trials."

#### Concept 5: Work Trials: The Ultimate Signal
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/adding-a-work-trial-to-your-interview-process.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share the core insight from James Hawkins (PostHog): "It makes it obvious who to hire. It is frequently surprising how someone performs relative to what we thought in the interviews." The spectrum runs from at-home projects (37signals, 7 days) to real co-working in the actual codebase (Linear, 3-5 days; Gumroad, 4-6 weeks). Key design principles from Karri Saarinen (Linear): "The goal is to simulate a normal working environment as much as possible." Linear pays $600/day regardless of seniority. They assemble a supporting team, do a kickoff, check-ins, and a final presentation. Jason Fried (37signals) keeps the prompt strategically vague: "I want to see how people choose to spend their time and what they think is important." Evaluation is group discussion with one decider. At Linear: "Anything other than a strong yes is a no." Share the convincing insight from Cristina Cordova (Linear): "Many people generally don't need much convincing. That is one of the myths."
3. **Apply it:** "Given your company size and hiring volume, where would a work trial fit on this spectrum? Could you start with a focused 1-day 'SuperDay' like PostHog, or would a longer co-working trial like Linear's be feasible?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What's Jason Fried's reasoning for keeping the work trial prompt vague? What is he actually testing for?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "candidates won't do this," push: "Cristina Cordova at Linear calls that a myth. PostHog says paying candidates makes it feel reasonable. The real filter is: if they won't invest this time, they're probably not motivated enough. What would you need to change to make it work?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 5 of 7. You have the interview toolkit. Next: the special case of hiring for growth — a discipline where the wrong hire can set you back a year."

#### Concept 6: Hiring for Growth
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/six-rules-of-hiring-for-growth.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Elena Verna's six rules. Rule 1: Growth model before growth — define your growth levers (acquisition, retention, monetization) and growth motions (product-led, sales-led, marketing-led) BEFORE hiring. Rule 2: Invest in data first. Rule 3: Create the first growth model yourself — don't outsource it to a Head of Growth, or you'll get a copy-paste from their last company. Rule 4: Hire a builder first (Growth PM, Growth Marketer), not an innovator (VP) or optimizer (specialist). Rule 5: Prioritize homegrown talent — growth leaders need internal support and context. Almost nobody starts their career in growth; the best came from analytics, engineering, product, or marketing. Rule 6: Let your growth model dictate where growth sits — don't hire a growth marketer to solve a product-led motion. Then load `newsletters/how-to-hire-your-first-growth-team.md` and share the timing guidance: pre-PMF, don't hire for growth. Finding PMF, do founder-led growth. Scaling PMF, hire your first dedicated growth roles — anchor the skill set to your biggest bottleneck.
3. **Apply it:** "What is your current growth model? Which lever is experiencing the most friction? And based on that, what type of growth hire should you be looking for?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why does Elena Verna say you should never outsource the first growth model to a Head of Growth hire?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "we need a Head of Growth to figure this out," push: "That's exactly Elena's mistake #3. The Head of Growth will take 6-12 months to understand your business and will likely copy-paste from their last company. The founding team needs to build the first growth model. What's stopping you?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 6 of 7. Almost there. Last concept: when you're ready to bring in senior leadership — how to avoid the most expensive hiring mistakes."

#### Concept 7: Hiring Your First Senior Leader
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `podcasts/lauren-ipsen.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lauren Ipsen's core insight from placing 80+ senior product leaders: the biggest mistake is chasing "shiny objects" — big names from Google or YouTube who are far from the work. "Just because this is the best talent, that doesn't necessarily mean they are the best talent for this role today." Look for someone closer to the work who can step up — some days operating like a PM, other days building from a leadership perspective. Her framework: define the mandate before you search. Work backwards from "if this person is crushing it, what does that look like?" Three archetypes: platform/infrastructure, core/consumer product, and growth/monetization specialists. Start with "head of" not "CPO" — don't over-promise titles. Then load `podcasts/gokul-rajaram.md` and share Gokul's advice: join (and hire from) the company that's going to be the #1 player in its segment. "I would much rather be the number two person at the leader in a space than the top person at a tier-two company." And from Luc Levesque: always keep a pulse on the market — "You never want to put yourself in a position where you have no idea what good looks like."
3. **Apply it:** "Are you hiring a senior leader right now or planning to? If so, what's the specific mandate — not just 'head of product' but what does success look like in 90 days, in a year?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What's Lauren Ipsen's argument against hiring the most impressive-sounding executive you can find? What should you optimize for instead?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they're drawn to big-name executives, push: "Lauren says those people often have a team of executors doing the real work. What you need is someone with a chip on their shoulder who wants to build. Can you describe that person?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's all 7 concepts. Time to bring it all together."

## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet
- **The First 10 Hires Sequence** (Lenny Rachitsky): Engineers first, then customer success/subject-matter experts, then sales/PMs/recruiters by hire #10. 100% of companies hired at least one engineer in first three.
- **Five Pillars of Closing** (Multiple founders): Captivating vision, A++ early team, putting in the time (~100 hours/hire), founder's network, love-bombing at offer stage.
- **$300-500k ARR Rule** (Multiple founders): Start with founder-led sales; hire your first "hungry senior AE" at $300-500k ARR when sales calls become repeatable and boring.
- **Signs of Excellence** (Luc Levesque): Look for repeated success across someone's life — awards, founding things, being poached by a former boss. Three signs of excellence is the bar.
- **The Shiny Object Trap** (Lauren Ipsen): The best talent in the world is not necessarily the best talent for this role today. Hire someone closer to the work who wants to build.
- **Six Rules of Hiring for Growth** (Elena Verna): Growth model before growth hires. Invest in data. Build the model yourself. Hire builders first. Prioritize homegrown talent. Let the model dictate org placement.
- **Innovators-Builders-Optimizers** (Elena Verna): Three growth profiles. Start with Builders (generalists who create systems). Move to Optimizers if model works, Innovators if it doesn't.
- **Work Trial Spectrum** (Multiple companies): From at-home projects (37signals, 7 days) to real co-working (Linear, 3-5 days; Gumroad, 4-6 weeks). "It makes it obvious who to hire" (James Hawkins, PostHog).
- **The Project Test** (Merci Grace): PM interview projects are the most predictive component — "performance on the project was closely related to their success at Slack over the course of years."
- **23-to-1 Funnel** (Ashby data): You need to talk to an average of 23 PM candidates to hire one great one. Plan your sourcing accordingly.
- **The Hogwash Question** (Nikhyl Singhal): "What's something everyone takes for granted that you think is hogwash?" Breaks interview performance mode and tests authentic opinionated thinking.
