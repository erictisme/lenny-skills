# /lenny-onboarding - User Onboarding & Activation

A structured coaching journey through designing onboarding that actually activates users — from defining your activation metric, to building the right friction, personalizing the first-run experience, choosing your entry model, and connecting onboarding to long-term retention.

## Prerequisites
- Lenny's Data MCP connected (`mcp.lennysdata.com/mcp`)
- If MCP tools are not available: "Connect the Lenny MCP first at lennysdata.com"

## About This Journey
You'll learn how to design onboarding as a retention engine, not a product tour. Starting with how to find and measure your activation metric, you'll work through the counterintuitive role of friction, how to personalize onboarding by user type, when to use freemium vs. trial vs. reverse trial, how PLG changes the onboarding equation, and how to connect it all back to long-term retention. Based on 11 articles and podcast episodes from Lenny's archive featuring onboarding experts like Lauryn Isford (Airtable), Laura Schaffer (Amplitude/Twilio), Bobby Pinero (Equals), Hila Qu (GitLab/Reforge), Rahul Vohra (Superhuman), Ben Williams (Snyk), and Lenny Rachitsky.

Best for: PMs, growth leads, and founders at B2B SaaS or consumer products who have a working product but are losing too many users between signup and first value — especially those who've been removing friction or adding tooltips without seeing activation rates move.

## The Curriculum
1. **Define Your Activation Metric** — How to find your product's real "aha moment," use regression analysis to prove it drives retention, and set a metric that's both predictive and actionable. Why a lower activation rate (5-15%) with high retention correlation beats a higher rate with weak correlation. *(Sources: "What is a good activation rate," "How to determine your activation metric," Lauryn Isford podcast)*
2. **Good Friction vs. Bad Friction** — Why removing all friction can destroy your business. How adding signup questions at Twilio improved conversion by 5%, why Equals' no-friction freemium nearly killed them, and the "pilling a hot dog" technique for embedding scary steps inside comfortable ones. *(Sources: Laura Schaffer podcast, "Lessons from going freemium: a decision that broke our business")*
3. **Design the First-Run Experience** — Airtable's three-bucket onboarding system (guided onboarding, personalization, ongoing education) that drove a 20% lift in activation. Why you should segment by learning style not job title, why naming features in tooltips fails, and Superhuman's case for manual onboarding. *(Sources: Lauryn Isford podcast, Rahul Vohra podcast, "How to increase your product's retention")*
4. **Choose Your Entry Model** — Freemium vs. trial vs. reverse trial. When each works, when each fails, and Lauryn Isford's case for doing both. How to decide what to make free and what to gate. *(Sources: "Freemium vs. trial," Lauryn Isford podcast, "Lessons from going freemium: a decision that broke our business")*
5. **PLG Onboarding: From User Aha to Team Aha** — Hila Qu's framework of four aha moments (user, team, buyer, paid-customer) and why most companies stop at the first one. How Snyk's activation metric "F30D" (fix in 30 days) drove retention, and how GitLab defined "2+ users and 2+ features within 14 days" as their team aha moment. *(Sources: "Five steps to starting your product-led growth motion," Hila Qu podcast, Ben Williams podcast)*
6. **Connect Onboarding to Retention** — Onboarding is the second-highest-leverage retention lever after improving the product itself. The seven strategies for increasing retention, why Andrew Chen says the real levers are in the new user experience, and how to build a multi-channel activation system (product + email + human touch). *(Sources: "How to increase your product's retention," "What is a good activation rate," "Five steps to starting your product-led growth motion")*

## Coaching Session Instructions

When this skill is invoked, follow this exact flow:

### Opening
1. Give a brief, motivating overview: what we're learning and why it matters. 3-4 sentences. Make the user feel excited, not overwhelmed.
2. Show the roadmap: "We'll cover 6 concepts. Here's the journey: (1) Define your activation metric, (2) Good friction vs. bad friction, (3) Design the first-run experience, (4) Choose your entry model, (5) PLG onboarding: from user aha to team aha, (6) Connect onboarding to retention. By the end, you'll be able to define your activation metric, design an onboarding flow that drives activation, and choose the right entry model for your product."
3. Ask: "What's your product, how do new users currently sign up and get started, and what percentage of signups would you estimate actually become regular users?"
4. Wait for their answer. Use it to personalize the entire session.

### For Each Concept (walk through in order, one at a time)

#### Concept 1: Define Your Activation Metric
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/what-is-a-good-activation-rate.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny and Yuriy Timen's findings from surveying 500+ products: the average activation rate is 34%, median is 25%. A good activation metric must be two things: (1) highly predictive of long-term retention (users who hit it retain at least 2x better) and (2) highly actionable (something your growth team can directly impact). The most common mistake is setting it too early (just signing up) or too late (multiple purchases). Then load `newsletters/how-to-determine-your-activation-metric.md` and share the three-step process: brainstorm potential aha moments, run regression to find which correlate with retention, run experiments to prove causation. Share the real examples: Slack's early metric was a team with 2+ users sending 50+ messages in 7 days. Figma's was collaboration in the same file within 24 hours. Airtable's was W4MUA (week 4 multi-user active). Then share Lauryn Isford's counterintuitive insight from `podcasts/lauryn-isford.md`: "An activation rate that falls in a lower percentage range, maybe 5 to 15%, is better than one that falls in a higher range because it means there's likely much higher correlation with long-term retention."
3. **Apply it:** "Based on your product, what do you think your user's real aha moment is — the earliest point where they experience your core value? And have you ever measured whether users who hit that moment actually retain better?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why does Lauryn Isford argue that a 5-15% activation rate can be better than a 40% activation rate? What's the reasoning?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "our aha moment is completing signup" or something equally early, push back: "As Lenny's survey found, setting activation too early is the most common mistake. Signup alone doesn't show value. For Slack it was team messaging, for Figma it was real-time collaboration. What's the moment your user first gets the real value your product delivers?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 1 of 6. You know what to measure. Next: the counterintuitive truth about friction in onboarding."

#### Concept 2: Good Friction vs. Bad Friction
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `podcasts/laura-schaffer.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Laura Schaffer's Twilio experiment: she added four dropdown questions to the signup flow (coding language, use case, product interest, developer vs. non-developer) and it improved conversion by 5%. The conventional wisdom was "developers hate friction, don't put anything in their way." But the questions were comforting — as Laura puts it, "When users are signing up for your product for the first time, it's new. That means it's scary. They're expecting it to be difficult." The questions helped users feel "I'm in the right place, this is going to do what I need it to do." Then share her "pilling a hot dog" insight: at Twilio, making "get a phone number" step one of onboarding killed conversion because telecom was the scary unfamiliar part. When they embedded the phone number step inside a familiar code-first docs experience, conversion jumped. As Laura says: "Bad friction is bad, and good friction is good. There's no such thing as all friction being bad." Then load `newsletters/lessons-from-going-freemium-a-decision-that-broke-our-business.md` and share Bobby Pinero's Equals story: they removed all friction (went freemium, let users skip data source setup) and it nearly destroyed the business. They 4x'd signups but couldn't retain anyone. Bobby's key insight: "The goal of onboarding is not for people to complete onboarding. It's for people to get their first moments of value. And removing friction is actually detached from this goal." When they added friction back (credit card required, data source required), engaged users went UP.
3. **Apply it:** "Think about your current signup flow. Are there places where you removed friction that might have actually removed the motivation to do the hard work needed to reach value? And are there questions you could ask early that would help users feel they're in the right place?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Bobby Pinero says 'the allure of seeing a new product is the strongest motivator new users have to complete setup tasks.' What does that mean practically for how you sequence your onboarding?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they default to "all friction is bad," push back with Laura's framework: "Laura Schaffer's takeaway is that the psyche of the user is as important as the product itself. Friction that reduces anxiety or increases commitment is good. Friction that blocks value delivery is bad. The question isn't how much friction — it's whether the friction serves the user's journey to value."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 2 of 6. You understand when to add and when to remove friction. Next: how to actually design the first-run experience."

#### Concept 3: Design the First-Run Experience
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `podcasts/lauryn-isford.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lauryn Isford's three-bucket approach that drove a 20% lift in Airtable's activation rate over six to eight months: (1) Guided onboarding — an immersive wizard where users answer questions on the left and see their workflow visualized on the right, reducing cognitive load and making progress visible. This was the single most impactful change. (2) Personalization — but by learning style and building style, NOT by job title. As Lauryn found, "knowing how someone would go about building was more effective than what do you actually want to see on the other side." (3) Ongoing education — "The Mole," a design pattern that pops up from the bottom of the screen with contextual tips as users explore. They retired most tooltips in favor of this. Share her two critical traps to avoid: naming features (telling users "This is automations" when they don't even know if automations matter to them) and building what employees think customers want rather than what customers actually need. Then load `podcasts/rahul-vohra.md` and share Rahul Vohra's manual onboarding approach at Superhuman: they had 20 people doing one-on-one concierge onboarding, and it was profitable. Vohra's reasoning: "The user metrics are excellent for engagement, retention, PMF score, NPS, virality. I think you'll significantly beat your industry benchmarks if you go to the effort of one-on-one onboarding your early customers." The manual approach also let them skip building a first-time user experience and focus all engineering on finding product-market fit. Then share Casey Winters' principle from `newsletters/how-to-increase-your-products-retention.md`: "The first principle we learned at Pinterest is that we should get people to the core product as fast as possible — but not faster."
3. **Apply it:** "Which of Lauryn's three buckets is weakest in your current onboarding? And are you segmenting users at all right now — by learning style, use case, or something else?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why did Lauryn say that segmenting by learning style worked better than segmenting by job title for Airtable's onboarding personalization?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they propose adding more tooltips, redirect with Lauryn's insight: "Airtable actually retired most of their tooltips. Tooltips name features — they announce what the company built. But users at this stage need to understand how to get value, not what features exist. The guided wizard approach lets users see progress as they build, which is fundamentally different from pointing at buttons."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 3 of 6. Halfway there. You know how to design the experience. Next: which entry model to wrap it in."

#### Concept 4: Choose Your Entry Model
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/freemium-vs-trial.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's analysis of 50+ SaaS pricing models: nearly 90% of freemium products also offer a trial. Go trial (not freemium) if your product needs hand-holding and has a high price point. Go freemium + trial if self-service works. Only Workday, ADP, Superhuman, Stripe, and MongoDB had no free offering at all. What to keep free: features that enable spread (invites, sharing), features that hook users (your aha features), features needed for retention (meaningful usage limits). What to charge for: professional/team needs (admin, support), power user features, increased usage limits. Then load `podcasts/lauryn-isford.md` and share Lauryn Isford's case for the reverse trial: offer both freemium AND a time-limited trial of premium features. The free trial is your moment to "showcase everything that can be done with your really cool product." Users experience premium value, then when the trial expires, they keep the free tier but know what they're missing. Then load `newsletters/lessons-from-going-freemium-a-decision-that-broke-our-business.md` and share Bobby Pinero's five diagnostic questions before going freemium: (1) Do you have a massive potential user base? (2) Do you have a really short time to value? (3) Is your product foundational for the end user? (4) Do you have very low incremental cost to serve? (5) Do free users contribute to your growth model through viral loops? Bobby's Equals failed on questions 2, 3, and 4.
3. **Apply it:** "Run through Bobby's five questions for your product. Where do you fall? And would a reverse trial — freemium with an auto-starting premium trial — make sense for your situation?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What's the difference between a reverse trial and a regular free trial? And why does Lauryn argue that the reverse trial is better for long-term user growth?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they default to "we should just go freemium because Notion and Figma do it," push back with Bobby's lesson: "Bobby Pinero thought the same thing at Equals — 'Let's get people into the product and figure out monetization later.' But for products with long time-to-value and high cost to serve, freemium can create a mass of unactivated users who never convert. The model has to match your product's activation dynamics."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 4 of 6. You know which entry model fits. Next: how PLG changes the onboarding equation — especially for B2B."

#### Concept 5: PLG Onboarding: From User Aha to Team Aha
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/five-steps-to-starting-your-product-led-growth-motion.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Hila Qu's framework of four distinct aha moments in B2B PLG: (1) User aha moment — an individual user hits value via product usage. Most companies stop here. (2) Team aha moment — the user invites teammates and they collaborate. At GitLab, this was "2+ users and 2+ features within the first 14 days." (3) Buyer aha moment — the team champion presents to the decision maker, who sees clear ROI. (4) Paid-customer aha moment — the paying customer reaches the aha moment again on the paid version, answering "Is this worth it?" As Hila puts it, product-led activation is "product-led, not product-only" — you should use product onboarding + lifecycle emails + human assistance + documentation as a multi-channel activation system. Then load `podcasts/ben-williams.md` and share Ben Williams' Snyk story: their activation metric was "F30D" — a team fixing a security vulnerability using Snyk within 30 days. Teams that did this had very strong likelihood of retention and higher propensity to purchase. Interestingly, it took just a single fix to establish the habit. Then share how Snyk's GitHub pull request loop served as both an acquisition AND activation loop — new users connected repos, Snyk auto-created branded fix PRs, other developers saw and followed links, creating a continuous cycle.
3. **Apply it:** "Which of Hila's four aha moments have you defined for your product? If you're B2B, do you have a team-level activation metric, or are you only tracking individual user activation?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why does Hila Qu say most B2B companies make a mistake by stopping at the user aha moment? What happens when you don't design for the team aha moment?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "we just need each user to get value," push back for B2B: "Ben Williams at Snyk puts it well — 'Unless the team realizes value, the product won't see sufficient adoption and monetization will be unlikely.' For multi-player B2B products, individual activation isn't enough. You need to think about what the team-level behavior is that predicts long-term retention."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 5 of 6. Almost there. Last concept: connecting everything back to the metric that matters most — retention."

#### Concept 6: Connect Onboarding to Retention
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/how-to-increase-your-products-retention.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's seven levers for increasing retention: (1) improve your product, (2) improve your onboarding, (3) make it stickier, (4) catch users before they leave, (5) remind users of your value, (6) bring back users after they've gone, (7) change your users. Onboarding is the second-highest-leverage lever. As Andrew Chen puts it: "The standard advice of listening to long-term customers who are already retained, and adding features for them — that doesn't work. The real levers to improve retention dramatically are in the experience for new users." Then load `newsletters/what-is-a-good-activation-rate.md` and share the eight most effective tactics for increasing activation from Lenny's 500+ product survey: (1) simpler onboarding UI/UX, (2) reducing onboarding friction, (3) emails and follow-up comms, (4) optimizing copy, (5) smarter top-of-funnel targeting, (6) sales outreach, (7) adding incentives, (8) showing value earlier. Note the balance: some of these add friction (sales outreach, smarter targeting), some remove it (simpler UX). The key is which friction serves the path to value. Then share Scott Belsky's principle: "The users of your product don't want to make choices, especially in the first mile. The default options you provide make all the difference in pulling new users through the first mile." And Hila Qu's multi-channel approach from `newsletters/five-steps-to-starting-your-product-led-growth-motion.md`: the ideal PLG activation experience is "led by product, powered by data, and supported by automated and low-touch channels, with high-touch assistance available to your high-value customers."
3. **Apply it:** "Of those eight tactics, which two or three do you think would have the biggest impact on your product right now? And which of the seven retention levers are you currently under-investing in?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why does Andrew Chen say the biggest retention levers are in the new user experience, not in features for existing users? What's the logic behind that?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they focus only on product improvements for existing users, redirect: "Lenny's data shows that you're more likely to impact retention by improving onboarding than by adding features for existing users. Why? Because it's much easier to get more people to experience value you've already created than to invent new value. The users who leave after Day 1 never even saw what your retained users love."
6. **Signpost:** "That's all 6 concepts. Time to bring it all together."

## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet
- **Activation Rate Formula** (Lenny + Yuriy Timen): activation rate = users who hit activation milestone / users who completed signup. Average across 500+ products is 34%.
- **Three-Step Activation Process** (Lenny + Yuriy Timen): (1) brainstorm aha moments, (2) correlate with retention via regression, (3) prove causation via experiments.
- **Good Friction / Bad Friction** (Laura Schaffer): bad friction blocks value delivery; good friction reduces user anxiety and increases commitment. "The psyche of the user is so critical."
- **Pilling a Hot Dog** (Laura Schaffer): embed scary unfamiliar steps inside comfortable familiar experiences. Don't lead with the thing that triggers the user's "bogeyman."
- **The Goal of Onboarding** (Bobby Pinero): "The goal of onboarding is not for people to complete onboarding. It's for people to get their first moments of value."
- **Three-Bucket Onboarding** (Lauryn Isford): (1) guided onboarding wizard, (2) personalization by learning/building style, (3) ongoing contextual education ("The Mole"). Drove 20% activation lift at Airtable.
- **Reverse Trial** (Lauryn Isford): offer freemium + a time-limited trial of premium features simultaneously. Users experience full value, then decide.
- **Four Aha Moments** (Hila Qu): user aha, team aha, buyer aha, paid-customer aha. Most companies only design for the first one.
- **F30D** (Ben Williams / Snyk): "Fix in 30 Days" — teams that fixed a vulnerability within 30 days retained strongly. Just one fix was enough to establish the habit.
- **W4MUA** (Lauryn Isford / Airtable): Week 4 Multi-User Active — the team activation metric. Multiple users collaborating in week 4 predicted long-term retention.
- **Seven Retention Levers** (Lenny): improve product, improve onboarding, make it stickier, catch users before they leave, remind of value, bring back churned users, change your users.
- **New User Experience Primacy** (Andrew Chen): "The real levers to improve retention dramatically are in the experience for new users" — not features for existing customers.
