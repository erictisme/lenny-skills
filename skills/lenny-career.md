# /lenny-career - Career Growth & Getting Promoted

A structured coaching journey through accelerating your career — from understanding what companies actually evaluate, to building the skills that get you promoted, to leveraging mentors and power, to negotiating comp that reflects your value.

## Prerequisites
- Lenny's Data MCP connected (`mcp.lennysdata.com/mcp`)
- If MCP tools are not available: "Connect the Lenny MCP first at lennysdata.com"

## About This Journey
You'll learn the concrete mechanics of career growth: how leveling frameworks actually work, what separates senior ICs from junior ones, how to build a collaborative relationship with your manager that drives promotions, how to build influence and power, how to leverage mentors, and how to negotiate compensation. Based on 25+ articles and podcast episodes from Lenny's archive featuring frameworks from Ethan Evans (Amazon VP), Jackie Bavaro (Asana), Deb Liu (Ancestry CEO), Nikhyl Singhal (Meta, Google), Jeffrey Pfeffer (Stanford), Jules Walter (YouTube, Slack), Julie Zhuo (Facebook), Bangaly Kaba (YouTube, Instagram), Wes Kao (Maven), and comp negotiation experts Niya Dragova and Jacob Warwick.

Best for: Mid-career PMs (2-5 years in) who feel stuck at their current level, senior ICs deciding between the IC track and management, or product leaders preparing for a Director+ role and want to be intentional about their trajectory rather than hoping good work speaks for itself.

## The Curriculum
1. **Know What You're Measured On** — The 10 core PM skills companies actually evaluate, how career ladders work across 20+ companies, and the three questions that tell you if you're doing a good job. *(Sources: "Skills PMs need to build," "Product management career ladders," "How to know if you're doing a good job as a product manager")*
2. **Level Up to Senior** — The three differentiators of senior PMs (strategy, autonomy, nuance), what "doing the job before you have the job" really means, and the five shifts that happen as you level up. *(Sources: "Becoming a senior Product Manager" by Jackie Bavaro, "How to get promoted," Jackie Bavaro podcast)*
3. **The Magic Loop** — Ethan Evans's five-step framework for systematically growing your career by building a collaborative relationship with your manager: do your job well, ask how you can help, do what they ask, ask for work that grows your career, repeat. *(Sources: "The Magic Loop," Ethan Evans podcast)*
4. **Build Influence and Power** — Jeffrey Pfeffer's 7 Rules of Power, Deb Liu on owning your career growth as an introvert, Wes Kao on managing up, and Claire Vo on "bending the universe in your favor." *(Sources: Jeffrey Pfeffer podcast, Deb Liu podcast, Wes Kao podcast, Claire Vo podcast)*
5. **Leverage Mentors and Sponsors** — Jules Walter's framework for finding, approaching, and getting the most from mentors, plus the critical difference between mentors and sponsors. *(Sources: Jules Walter podcast, "Navigating your early career")*
6. **The IC-to-Manager Decision** — What the jump from Senior PM to manager of PMs actually looks like, the five unexpected jobs of a PM manager, and how to decide if management is right for you. *(Sources: "Moving from IC product manager to manager of product managers," Julie Zhuo podcast, Nikhyl Singhal podcast)*
7. **Negotiate Your Comp** — The GAINS framework for comp negotiation, the 10 commandments of salary negotiation, and why most people leave money on the table even when they think they're negotiating well. *(Sources: "The ultimate guide to negotiating your comp," "The 10 commandments of salary negotiation")*

## Coaching Session Instructions

When this skill is invoked, follow this exact flow:

### Opening
1. Give a brief, motivating overview: what we're learning and why it matters. 3-4 sentences. Make the user feel excited, not overwhelmed.
2. Show the roadmap: "We'll cover 7 concepts. Here's the journey: (1) Know what you're measured on, (2) Level up to senior, (3) The Magic Loop, (4) Build influence and power, (5) Leverage mentors and sponsors, (6) The IC-to-manager decision, (7) Negotiate your comp. By the end, you'll have a concrete plan for your next promotion and know how to negotiate the comp you deserve."
3. Ask: "What's your current role and level, how long have you been at this level, and what does 'the next step' look like for you — is it a promotion, a role change, a company change, or something else?"
4. Wait for their answer. Use it to personalize the entire session.

### For Each Concept (walk through in order, one at a time)

#### Concept 1: Know What You're Measured On
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/skills-pms-need-to-build.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's research across 20+ PM career ladders. The top 10 PM skills, in order: (1) Leadership (85% of companies), (2) Execution (75%), (3) Strategy (75%), (4) Communication (60%), (5) Deliver impact, (6) Leverage customer insights and data, (7) Planning and goal setting, (8) Collaboration, (9) Vision, (10) Ownership. Then share what separates junior from senior PMs across these ladders — five shifts: increase your scope (feature to product to product line), get more proactive (participate to initiate to challenge), deliver impact consistently, build trust to become more autonomous, and mentor and lead. Then load `newsletters/how-to-know-if-youre-doing-a-good-job-as-a-product-manager.md` and share the three-question self-assessment: (1) Is your team delivering business impact? (2) Are YOU personally contributing to the team's success? (3) Do stakeholders regard you highly? As Lenny puts it: "If 100% of people love you, you may not be pushing hard enough. I'd aim for 80% of people loving you, 5-10% feeling uneasy, and occasionally a few people being like WTF."
3. **Apply it:** "Based on the top 10 skills list, which 2-3 are your strongest, and which 1-2 are most holding you back from the next level? And on the three-question self-assessment, where would you honestly score yourself?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What are the five shifts that happen as a PM levels up from junior to senior, according to the career ladder research?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they focus only on execution, push: "Execution is table stakes at the senior level. The career ladders show that strategy, leadership, and impact become the differentiators. Which of those three is your biggest gap?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 1 of 7. You know the scorecard. Next: the specific playbook for leveling up to Senior PM."

#### Concept 2: Level Up to Senior
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/becoming-a-senior-product-manager.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Jackie Bavaro's framework. Senior PMs don't need to be much better at the work a regular PM does — the biggest differences are behind the scenes. The three differentiators: (1) Strategy — "A senior PM is responsible for developing and evangelizing a strategy that leads to meaningful customer and business success." They don't prioritize by incremental value; they prioritize by how much it moves the product toward the long-term vision. Three parts: vision, strategic framework, roadmap. (2) Autonomy — "Senior PMs are able to run a team independently." The template for sharing your work: "Hi manager, let me tell you about a challenge I ran into ___. Here's how I'm handling it ___. Any thoughts?" This shows the challenge, your judgment, AND invites feedback. (3) Nuance — "The more senior you get, the more you'll be faced with decisions where the right answer is 'it depends.'" Senior PMs can lay out their thought process in minutes because of rich mental models. Then load `newsletters/how-to-get-promoted.md` and share Lenny's 7 strategies: deliver more impact, take on more scope, demonstrate you've addressed a gap, find an influential champion, pay attention to who gets promoted, ask for it, or quit. The most underappreciated strategy: "The most powerful ingredient in getting promoted is to have someone with influence fight for you in the calibration process."
3. **Apply it:** "Of Jackie's three differentiators — strategy, autonomy, nuance — which one is your weakest? And of Lenny's 7 strategies, which ones have you actually tried?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Explain Jackie Bavaro's autonomy template in your own words. Why does this format work better than just asking your manager for advice?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "I just need to do better work," push: "Lenny says the most important ingredient has always been doing good work, but the process often needs a nudge. Great work alone isn't enough — you also need a champion, you need to address your specific gaps, and you need to ask for the promotion. Which of those nudges are you missing?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 2 of 7. You know the target. Next: the most systematic framework for actually getting there — The Magic Loop."

#### Concept 3: The Magic Loop
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/the-magic-loop.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Ethan Evans's five-step framework in full. Step 1: Do your current job well — verify with your manager that you're doing well before asking for more. Step 2: Ask your manager how you can help them — "Managers always need help. Simply the act of offering help will make you stand out." Step 3: Do what they ask, even if it's not glamorous — "If you really want to get the most impact, take on work that others may be avoiding." Step 4: Ask your manager how you can help in a way that also grows your skills toward a career goal — "After you have first helped out the manager, they are more likely to help you in return." Step 5: Repeat. Then share the advanced forms: (1) Suggest your own ideas instead of just asking, (2) "Just do it" — see what needs doing and do it. As Ethan puts it: "Your career is like a card game, where you are slowly trying to play or discard your less useful cards while getting new, higher cards." The magic relies on simple human nature: "We have a natural sense of fair play and partnership, and we want to invest in people we view as having our best interests at heart."
3. **Apply it:** "Think about your current relationship with your manager. Which step of the Magic Loop are you on right now? Have you ever explicitly asked your manager how you can help them?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why does Ethan Evans say you should help your manager BEFORE asking for career-advancing work? What's the human psychology behind this?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "my manager doesn't care about my growth," push: "Ethan addresses this directly. Start with yourself first — get broad feedback from peers. If you've genuinely tried the loop and your manager is exploitative, then it's time to find a new manager. But most people blame the manager without trying the loop first. Have you tried steps 1-3?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 3 of 7. You have the system. Next: building the influence and power that makes everything else work faster."

#### Concept 4: Build Influence and Power
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `podcasts/jeffrey-pfeffer.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Jeffrey Pfeffer's core thesis: "The reason why you should pay attention to power is because it leads to a lot of good things — salary, getting promoted, being happy in your career, being less stressed." His 7 Rules of Power: (1) Get out of your own way — stop believing the world is just and that good work alone will be rewarded. (2) Break the rules — "Rules are for the obedience of fools and the guidance of wise people." (3) Show up in a powerful fashion — first impressions, body language, energy. (4) Create a powerful brand — become known for something specific. (5) Network relentlessly — "Your network is your net worth." (6) Use your power — power unused atrophies. (7) Once you've acquired power, what you did to get it will be forgiven, forgotten, or both. Then load `podcasts/deb-liu.md` and share Deb Liu's insight for introverts: "Some of the best PMs I have ever worked with are terrible PMs for their career. They just drift from job to job." Her reframe: "It looks like self-promotion. But instead, what if I called it educating about all the great work your team has been doing?" Then load `podcasts/wes-kao.md` and share Wes Kao's managing-up framework: "Most people assume their boss has to manage them. If you embrace managing your boss, they're going to appreciate you much more, you're going to get more opportunities."
3. **Apply it:** "Which of Pfeffer's 7 rules makes you most uncomfortable? That's probably the one that would create the biggest unlock for you. And on Deb Liu's reframe — are you educating people about your team's work, or are you hoping it speaks for itself?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Jeffrey Pfeffer says 'get out of your own way' is rule number one. What does he mean by that, and why is it the first rule?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "I don't want to play politics," push: "Pfeffer's whole point is that this isn't about personality — these are skills that can be mastered. And not playing the game doesn't opt you out. It just means you lose by default. Deb Liu was an introvert who became CEO of Ancestry. She didn't change who she was — she reframed visibility as educating, not self-promoting. Can you try that reframe?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 4 of 7. Past the halfway mark. Next: how to systematically leverage mentors and sponsors."

#### Concept 5: Leverage Mentors and Sponsors
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `podcasts/jules-walter.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Jules Walter's mentorship framework. The critical distinction: mentors give advice, sponsors advocate for you. You need both, but sponsors are harder to get and more valuable for career growth. Jules's approach to getting feedback: "If you give me feedback, I'll be like, 'Hey, thank you so much. This is super helpful.' Inside my heart might be melting. But externally I'm like, 'Hey, thank you,' and I mean it." This is the key — people give more feedback to people who receive it well. His approach to finding mentors: don't ask someone to "be your mentor." Instead, ask specific questions, show you've done your homework, follow up on their advice, and report back on what happened. The relationship builds naturally. Then load `newsletters/navigating-your-early-career.md` and share Lenny's five strategies for early career navigation: (1) Don't over-optimize for the resume — "the pressure to keep building your resume never ends." (2) Follow your interests — but not in a vacuum. Find the intersection of what you enjoy, what you're good at, and what creates value for others. (3) When you're comfy for too long, shake things up. (4) Work with great people — "We are the average of the five people we spend the most time with." (5) Never stop learning.
3. **Apply it:** "Do you currently have mentors? More importantly, do you have a sponsor — someone with influence who would fight for you in a room you're not in? If not, who in your organization could become one?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What's the difference between a mentor and a sponsor, and why does Jules Walter say sponsors are harder to get?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "I don't have time for mentorship," push: "Jules Walter's whole point is that it doesn't have to be a formal commitment. Ask one specific question to someone you admire, follow up on their advice, and report back. That's 15 minutes total. The relationship compounds from there. Who could you reach out to this week?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 5 of 7. Next: the fork in the road that every senior IC faces — do you go into management?"

#### Concept 6: The IC-to-Manager Decision
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/moving-from-ic-product-manager-to-manager-of-product-managers.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's framework. As a PM manager, your job fundamentally changes: "Instead of working directly on the product, everything now happens indirectly, through your PM team." Andy Grove's formula: "A PM manager's output = the output of their team + the output of the neighboring teams under their influence." The five unexpected jobs: (1) Stopping stupid sh*t from happening, (2) Unblocking, unblocking, unblocking — "Anything that slows your team down is now your fault," (3) Preserving the PM team quality bar, (4) Preserving the product quality bar — "Push your team to think deeper and go further," (5) Building a strong and united leadership group. The five ways to get promoted to PM manager: demonstrate you can lead people, deliver, handle complexity, develop a winning vision/strategy, and ASK FOR IT. Then load `podcasts/nikhyl-singhal.md` and share Nikhyl's career chapters framework: your career has chapters, and what made you successful in one chapter may not serve you in the next. His dog-track metaphor: "The moment the dogs accidentally touched the rabbit, they would never run again." Don't build your career around chasing one destination — build it around always having something important and motivating to do.
3. **Apply it:** "Based on the five unexpected jobs of a PM manager — which ones excite you, and which ones sound draining? Your honest answer tells you a lot about whether management is right for you right now."
4. **Check understanding:** "What does Lenny mean when he says the PM manager's job is 'leverage'? And why is that fundamentally different from the IC PM job?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they assume management is the only path, push: "Lenny explicitly says 'you don't have to get into management.' Two-thirds of the companies he studied have a separate IC track that goes to Principal PM and beyond. The question isn't 'should I become a manager' but 'where do I create the most impact and get the most energy?' Which is it for you?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 6 of 7. One more to go — and it's the one most people wish they'd learned earlier: negotiating comp."

#### Concept 7: Negotiate Your Comp
1. **Load the source:** Use `mcp__lennysdata__read_content` with filename `newsletters/the-10-commandments-of-salary-negotiation.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Niya Dragova's 10 commandments. Commandment 1: Negotiation starts earlier than you think — when the recruiter asks for your salary expectations, do NOT give a number. Say: "Can you tell me the salary band for this level? Happy to let you know if it's within my range." Commandment 2: Mine for intel during interviews — ask about priorities, challenges, and org structure. Commandment 3: Don't give in to recruiter pressure to name a number. Commandment 4: At FAANG, your recruiter may have no say — a separate compensation committee sets numbers. Commandment 5: Read between the lines — a low initial offer means room for growth, middle-of-road means you got "the number" (80% of people do), top-of-band means there was likely a discussion about a higher level. Commandment 7: Win hearts and minds BEFORE negotiating — meet decision makers, come with ideas, make them sell YOU on the role. The biggest myth: "I need a competing offer." You don't. Being able to say you're speaking to other companies is sufficient. Then load `newsletters/the-ultimate-guide-to-negotiating-your-comp.md` and share Jacob Warwick's GAINS framework: Gather intelligence, Align with their needs, Influence key stakeholders, Navigate complexity, Secure your value. The key insight: "While most candidates ask 'What can you offer me?', top earners demonstrate 'Here's how I'll solve your most pressing challenges.'" This isn't about asking for money — it's about making your value so clear that comp becomes a reflection of it.
3. **Apply it:** "Think about your last comp negotiation. Did you give a number first? Did you mine for intel during interviews? And did you try to win hearts and minds before discussing money?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Explain why Niya Dragova says you should never give your salary number first, even when the recruiter says they'll 'go to bat for you.'"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say "I'm not good at negotiation," push: "Niya helped a senior FAANG executive negotiate $5.4M more on her offer. Even she didn't know the game. This isn't about being aggressive — it's about understanding the system. The largest increases come from winning hearts and minds before you ever discuss numbers. What would it look like to come to your next negotiation with ideas for the company instead of just a number?"
6. **Signpost:** "That's all 7 concepts. Time for your summary."

   - **This quarter:** Identify one potential sponsor and one potential mentor, and take the first step to build each relationship (`podcasts/jules-walter.md`)
3. For each step, cite which article/podcast backs it up with the source filename so they can read/listen in full.
4. Offer to save session notes. Ask the user where they'd like to save. Suggest `./lenny-sessions/[YYYY-MM-DD]-career.md` as default.

## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet
- **Top 10 PM Skills** (Lenny, from 20+ career ladders): Leadership, Execution, Strategy, Communication, Impact, Customer Insights, Planning, Collaboration, Vision, Ownership — in that order of importance.
- **Three Questions Self-Assessment** (Lenny): Is your team delivering business impact? Are YOU personally contributing? Do stakeholders regard you highly?
- **Strategy-Autonomy-Nuance** (Jackie Bavaro): The three differentiators of senior PMs — set the strategy, run the team independently, handle "it depends" decisions with nuance.
- **Seven Strategies for Getting Promoted** (Lenny): Deliver more impact, take on more scope, demonstrate you've addressed a gap, find an influential champion, pay attention to who gets promoted, ask for it, or quit.
- **The Magic Loop** (Ethan Evans): Five steps — do your job well, ask how you can help your manager, do what they ask, ask for work that grows your career, repeat. Advanced: suggest your own ideas, then just do it.
- **7 Rules of Power** (Jeffrey Pfeffer): Get out of your own way, break the rules, show up powerfully, create a powerful brand, network relentlessly, use your power, and what you did to get power will be forgiven.
- **Mentor vs. Sponsor** (Jules Walter): Mentors give advice. Sponsors advocate for you in rooms you're not in. You need both. Get more feedback by receiving it graciously.
- **PM Manager's Output Formula** (Andy Grove / Lenny): Your output = the output of your team + the output of neighboring teams under your influence.
- **Career Chapters** (Nikhyl Singhal): Your career has chapters. What made you successful in one chapter may not serve you in the next. Always have something motivating ahead.
- **GAINS Framework** (Jacob Warwick): Gather intelligence, Align with needs, Influence stakeholders, Navigate complexity, Secure value — for negotiating comp as a strategic process, not a single conversation.
- **10 Commandments of Salary Negotiation** (Niya Dragova): Never give your number first, mine for intel during interviews, win hearts and minds before negotiating, read between the lines of the initial offer.
- **Educate Don't Self-Promote** (Deb Liu): Reframe visibility from self-promotion to "educating about all the great work your team has been doing."
- **Managing Up** (Wes Kao): If you manage your boss, they'll appreciate you more, give you more opportunities, and build more trust with you. It's a skill, not a burden.
