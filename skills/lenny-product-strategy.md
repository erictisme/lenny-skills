# /lenny-product-strategy - Product Strategy

A structured coaching journey through the complete product strategy system — from defining what strategy actually is, to crafting a compelling vision, choosing where to play, building strategic pillars, prioritizing your roadmap, defending your big bets, and developing the product sense to tie it all together.

## About This Journey
You'll learn how to build, articulate, and execute a product strategy that creates focus, alignment, and measurable progress. Starting with the fundamentals of what strategy is (and isn't), you'll work through vision-setting, choosing where to play and how to win, an operator's playbook for crafting strategic pillars, prioritization frameworks that double your hit rate, how to defend long-term bets inside organizations, and developing the product sense that underpins all strategic thinking. Based on 30+ articles and podcast episodes from Lenny's archive featuring strategy legends Roger Martin, Richard Rumelt, Hamilton Helmer, and operators from Netflix, Airbnb, Figma, Meta, Asana, Dropbox, and more.

Best for: PMs at Series B+ companies who've been told to "be more strategic" but don't know where to start, product leaders tasked with writing their first strategy document, or founders transitioning from execution mode to needing a real plan for the next 2 years.

## The Curriculum
1. **What Strategy Actually Is** — Why most "strategies" are just goals in disguise. The kernel of good strategy: diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent action. What separates playing to win from playing to play. *(Sources: Richard Rumelt podcast, Roger Martin podcast, "Getting better at product strategy")*
2. **The Strategy Stack: Mission to Task** — How mission, vision, strategy, goals, roadmap, and tasks connect in a hierarchy. Where strategy sits. Why you need each layer. *(Sources: "Mission → Vision → Strategy → Goals → Roadmap → Task," Ebi Atawodi podcast on vision, Nickey Skarstad podcast on translating vision into goals)*
3. **Where to Play and How to Win** — Roger Martin's Strategy Choice Cascade: winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities, management systems. Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers and why you need a durable competitive advantage. *(Sources: Roger Martin podcast, Hamilton Helmer podcast, Gibson Biddle podcast on DHM framework)*
4. **Crafting Your Strategic Pillars** — Chandra Janakiraman's Strategy Blocks: the 5-step, 8-12 week operator's playbook for crafting a strategy. Small "s" (2-year, problem-forward) vs. big "S" (3-10 year, future-backward). How to score opportunity areas and select pillars. *(Sources: "Strategy Blocks: An operator's guide to product strategy," Chandra Janakiraman podcast, Jackie Bavaro podcast)*
5. **Prioritizing Your Roadmap** — RICE and DRICE frameworks for ruthless prioritization. Where great roadmap ideas come from. Now-Next-Later roadmapping. Why one team needs one roadmap. *(Sources: "Introducing DRICE: a modern prioritization framework," "Where Great Product Roadmap Ideas Come From," Janna Bastow podcast, Gibson Biddle podcast on GEM framework, "One team, one roadmap")*
6. **Defending Your Big Bets** — The 80/20 rule for balancing incremental wins with long-term bets. Creating "cover fire" to protect ambitious projects. How to avoid getting your resources pulled. *(Sources: "Defending your big bets," Mihika Kapoor podcast on building 0-to-1, Anneka Gupta podcast on becoming more strategic)*
7. **Developing Product Sense** — The meta-skill underneath strategy. Building empathy through user observation. Deconstructing products. Learning from great product thinkers. Catching technology shifts. *(Sources: "How to develop product sense," Julie Zhuo podcast, Scott Belsky podcast)*

## Coaching Session Instructions

When this skill is invoked, follow this exact flow:

### Opening
1. Give a brief, motivating overview: what we're learning and why it matters. 3-4 sentences. Make the user feel excited, not overwhelmed.
2. Show the roadmap: "We'll cover 7 concepts. Here's the journey: (1) What strategy actually is, (2) The strategy stack from mission to task, (3) Where to play and how to win, (4) Crafting your strategic pillars, (5) Prioritizing your roadmap, (6) Defending your big bets, (7) Developing product sense. By the end, you'll be able to diagnose whether your current strategy is real or just dressed-up goals, write a strategy document with defensible pillars, and prioritize a roadmap that your team actually believes in."
3. Ask: "What's your product, who are your users, and what does your current strategy look like — do you have a written strategy document, or is it more in people's heads? And have you ever gotten the feedback that you need to be 'more strategic'?"
4. Wait for their answer. Use it to personalize the entire session.

### For Each Concept (walk through in order, one at a time)

#### Concept 1: What Strategy Actually Is
1. **Load the source:** Use the Lenny MCP's `read_content` tool with filename `podcasts/richard-rumelt.md` to get the full article. (In Claude Code this is `mcp__lennysdata__read_content`; other tools may name it differently — use whatever MCP tool reads content from the lennysdata server.)
2. **Teach it:** Share Richard Rumelt's core insight: "A strategy is a design for overcoming a high-stakes challenge." Most strategies fail because they're just goals in disguise — Rumelt calls this "bad strategy." Bad strategy has three telltale signs: (1) mistaking goals for strategy ("our strategy is to grow 30%"), (2) missing a diagnosis of what's actually going on, and (3) fluff — fancy words that sound strategic but say nothing. Good strategy has a "kernel" of three elements: a diagnosis of the challenge, a guiding policy for how to deal with it, and coherent actions that implement that policy. Then load `podcasts/roger-martin.md` and share Martin's complementary definition: "Strategy is an integrated set of choices that compels desired customer action." His key insight is that strategy exists because we can't control what customers do — we can only make choices that compel them to choose us. Martin argues you're either playing to win (differentiated or lowest cost) or you're playing to play, and playing to play means you'll eventually get shoved out by competitors who are playing to win.
3. **Apply it:** "Look at your current strategy — does it have Rumelt's three elements? A clear diagnosis of the challenge, a guiding policy, and coherent actions? Or is it more of a goals list?"
4. **Check understanding:** "In your own words, what's the difference between a strategy and a set of goals?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they conflate strategy with OKRs or a project list, correct: "As Rumelt puts it, a strategy is not a list of goals. Goals are ambitions. Strategy is the plan for how you overcome the specific challenge standing between you and those goals. 'Grow revenue 30%' is an ambition. 'Win the mid-market by being the only product that does X, by investing in these three capabilities' — that's a strategy."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 1 of 7. You now have a diagnostic test for whether something is a real strategy or just goals in a nice font. Next: how strategy fits into the full stack from mission down to daily tasks."

#### Concept 2: The Strategy Stack: Mission to Task
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/mission--vision--strategy--goals--roadmap--task.md`
2. **Teach it:** Walk through Lenny's hierarchy: Mission (what are we trying to achieve?) → Vision (what does the world look like when we've achieved it?) → Strategy (what's our plan to win?) → Goals (how will we measure progress?) → Roadmap (what do we need to build?) → Task (one unit of work). Use the Tesla example: Mission is "Accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy." Vision is "Create the most compelling car company of the 21st century." Strategy is the four-step plan: build a sports car, use that revenue to build an affordable car, then an even more affordable car, while providing zero-emission power. Then load `podcasts/ebi-atawodi.md` and share Ebi Atawodi's insight on vision specifically: a vision must be (1) lofty — it should scare you in an exciting way, (2) realistic and attainable, (3) devoid of today's tech limitations, and (4) grounded in a clear user problem. She uses the "Once upon a time..." storytelling framework: Once upon a time [the world was like this]. Because of that [this was the problem]. And one day [we built this]. And because of that [the world is now like this].
3. **Apply it:** "Can you articulate each layer of the stack for your product? Mission, vision, strategy, goals, roadmap? Which layers are clear and which are fuzzy or missing?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What's the difference between vision and mission? And where does strategy sit relative to goals?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they mix up vision and mission, clarify: "Mission is WHY you exist — the purpose. Vision is WHAT IT LOOKS LIKE when you've succeeded. As Ebi puts it, if a pilot said 'I'm a great pilot but I don't know where we're going,' you'd get off the plane. The vision is the destination."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 2 of 7. You now have the full stack. Next: the hardest part — choosing where to play and how to win."

#### Concept 3: Where to Play and How to Win
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `podcasts/roger-martin.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Roger Martin's Strategy Choice Cascade — five questions every strategy must answer: (1) What is our winning aspiration? (2) Where will we play? (what customers, geographies, products, channels) (3) How will we win? (differentiation or low cost — there is no third option) (4) What capabilities must we have? (5) What management systems are required? Martin's key insight: 10% of S&P 500 CEOs are ex-Procter & Gamble because P&G trains people four levels down the org to make strategic choices. Strategy isn't just for CEOs. Then load `podcasts/hamilton-helmer.md` and share Helmer's complementary framework: strategy is about the fundamental determinants of business value. Power = a benefit (cost or price advantage) + a barrier (something durable competitors can't copy). He identifies 7 Powers, but for startups the key ones are counter-positioning, scale economies, switching costs, and network economies. Branding and process power come later. His key insight: "You should always be thinking about power — even before product-market fit, you're tilting probabilities." Then load `podcasts/gibson-biddle.md` and share Gibson Biddle's DHM model from Netflix: your product strategy should delight customers in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways. Netflix's hard-to-copy advantages: original content, brand trust, personalization technology, and scale.
3. **Apply it:** "For your product, can you answer Martin's five questions? And which of Helmer's powers could you realistically build? Where is your hard-to-copy advantage?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What are the only two ways to win, according to Martin? And why does Helmer say that having the best team or moving fastest is not a power?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say their advantage is "we move faster" or "we have a great team," push back: "As Helmer puts it, operational excellence is a treadmill — if you stop running, you get creamed, but it's not power because the things that drive it can be mimicked. Power requires a benefit AND a barrier."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 3 of 7. You now have two frameworks for choosing where to play and how to win. Next: the actual step-by-step process for crafting strategic pillars — the operator's playbook."

#### Concept 4: Crafting Your Strategic Pillars
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/strategy-blocks-an-operators-guide-to-product-strategy.md`
2. **Teach it:** Walk through Chandra Janakiraman's Strategy Blocks framework — the most actionable, step-by-step process for crafting a strategy. It has two parts: Small "s" strategy (2-year, present-forward, problem-solving) and Big "S" strategy (3-10 year, future-backward, aspirational). The small "s" process takes 8-12 weeks across 5 steps: (1) Preparation — assemble a strategy working group, gather behavioral insights, UXR insights, competitive analysis, and run leadership interviews asking "What does success look like? What does failure look like?" (2) Strategy Sprint — generate 50-150 problem statements, cluster into 10-15 themes, flip to opportunity framings, score each on expected impact, certainty, clarity of levers, and uniqueness of levers. The top 3 become your strategic pillars. (3) Design Sprint — create illustrative concepts like concept cars. (4) Document Writing — connect all pieces into a coherent strategy doc. (5) Rollout — 1:1s with gatekeepers, group review, team roadshows, then empower teams to build roadmaps. Then share Lenny's complementary advice from `newsletters/getting-better-at-product-strategy.md`: strategy requires getting three things right — (1) the actual strategy (problem-oriented, insight-driven, actionable, focused, cohesive), (2) articulating it (short, memorable, uses a framework or metaphor, follows the rule of three), and (3) acting on it (remind people constantly, run all prioritization through it, evolve it as you learn).
3. **Apply it:** "Which step of Chandra's process would be most valuable for you right now? Do you have the raw inputs — user insights, competitive analysis, leadership interviews — or do you need to start there?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What are the four scoring dimensions for selecting strategic pillars? And why does Chandra separate small 's' and big 'S' strategy?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they want to skip the preparation step, push back: "Chandra found that skipping prep is the most common mistake. Without behavioral insights, UXR, competitive analysis, and leadership interviews, you're selecting pillars based on opinion, not evidence. Even in a fast-track version, he compresses prep to 2 days — he never eliminates it."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 4 of 7. You now have a repeatable playbook for crafting a strategy. Next: once you have strategic pillars, how do you prioritize the specific things to build?"

#### Concept 5: Prioritizing Your Roadmap
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/introducing-drice-a-modern-prioritization-framework.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share the RICE and DRICE frameworks from Darius Contractor (ex-Dropbox, Facebook, Airtable) and Alexey Komissarouk. RICE is T-shirt sizing ideas on Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort — a 30-second estimate per idea. DRICE is "Detailed RICE" — a 30-minute deep dive per shortlisted idea that produces a specific dollar estimate. The Dropbox case study is key: the Business Migration Tool became the biggest activation win of the quarter, but without DRICE it would never have been prioritized. Teams using DRICE had TWICE the impact rate of teams using basic RICE. Then load `newsletters/where-great-product-roadmap-ideas-come-from.md` and share Lenny's 15 best sources for roadmap ideas — the top ones are talking to customers, observing them, using the product yourself, and working backwards from long-term vision. Where ideas rarely come from: large brainstorms, staying heads down too long, copying competitors. Then load `podcasts/janna-bastow.md` and share Janna Bastow's insight: "The value isn't in your roadmap — the value is in the roadmapping process." She invented Now-Next-Later as an alternative to date-based roadmaps — you're laying out assumptions about problems to solve and checking them with your team and customers.
3. **Apply it:** "How do you currently prioritize? Do you have a scoring framework, or is it more gut feel and loudest voice? And are you sourcing ideas broadly or mostly from internal brainstorms?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What's the difference between RICE and DRICE? And why did Dropbox teams using DRICE get twice the impact?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they say prioritization is just about picking the most impactful thing, push back: "Impact alone isn't enough. RICE balances impact with reach, confidence, and effort. And DRICE goes further — a 30-minute investigation often reveals that 'promising' ideas are defeated by the math, while 'nice to haves' turn out to be the biggest wins. The point is to replace gut feel with educated guesses."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 5 of 7. You now have a prioritization system. Next: once you have your roadmap, how do you protect the ambitious projects from getting killed?"

#### Concept 6: Defending Your Big Bets
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/defending-your-big-bets.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Lenny's core insight: a big bet is like an experimental weapon you're smuggling to the front. The forces of organizational gravity want it squashed. The solution is "cover fire" — consistently shipping small, incremental wins that move your KPIs while you work on the long-term bet in parallel. The rule of thumb: spend 80% of your team's time on short-term wins and 20% on high-risk, long-term bets. Why this works: (1) you don't give anyone a reason to mess with your team, (2) you build trust in your ability to execute, (3) you earn the right to take bigger bets. Share advice from practitioners: Sriram Krishnan (keep it small, avoid too much attention until ready), Jason Warner CTO of GitHub (code wins — demo an end-to-end prototype), Tomer London co-founder of Gusto (small hungry entrepreneurial team, clear mandate, independent operations). Then load `podcasts/mihika-kapoor.md` and share Mihika Kapoor's approach to building 0-to-1 at Figma: "Vision is everything — it's the anchor that makes every setback feel like forward progress." Her method: be inseparable from your users, create prototypes before a project gets the green light, use pain-point → solution → proof-point storytelling to get buy-in.
3. **Apply it:** "Do you have a big bet right now that's struggling to survive? What's your cover fire — are you shipping enough incremental wins to earn the right to take that risk?"
4. **Check understanding:** "Why does Lenny recommend the 80/20 split? And what does 'cover fire' mean in a product context?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they think they can just pitch the big bet and get full support, challenge: "As Lenny learned at Airbnb, even if you have leadership support today, people and priorities change. When there are new fires, non-essential projects get raided for resources. Cover fire means you're never the easiest target."
6. **Signpost:** "That's concept 6 of 7. Almost there. The final concept is the meta-skill that makes all of this work — product sense."

#### Concept 7: Developing Product Sense
1. **Load the source:** Use `read_content` with filename `newsletters/how-to-develop-product-sense.md`
2. **Teach it:** Share Jules Walter's definition: "Product sense is the skill of consistently being able to craft products that have the intended impact on their users." It relies on empathy (discovering meaningful user needs) and creativity (coming up with effective solutions). It's a LEARNED skill, not something you're born with. Four practices to build it: (1) Observe people interacting with products 2-4 times a month — attend user research sessions, pay attention to facial expressions, ask open-ended questions. At Slack, this revealed that users didn't understand what Slack did and didn't want to share their mobile address books. (2) Deconstruct everyday products — try new apps for 1-2 hours a month, compare competitors (like Cash App vs. Venmo), notice your own moments of frustration and delight. (3) Learn from great product thinkers — attend product reviews, take notes, look for patterns in feedback. Stewart Butterfield's key lesson: "The details are not the details. They make the design." (4) Be curious about technology shifts — live in the future and build what's missing (Paul Graham). Track macro shifts (platform changes, social shifts) and micro shifts (new APIs, new capabilities like WebGL that enabled Figma).
3. **Apply it:** "Which of the four practices do you currently do? Which one could you start this week?"
4. **Check understanding:** "What are the two components of product sense? And why does Jules say it's a learned skill, not an innate talent?"
5. **Hold the bar:** If they think product sense is just about having good taste, correct: "Product sense isn't taste — it's empathy plus creativity, both of which are built through deliberate practice. Jules built his by observing hundreds of users at Slack and deconstructing products every month. Stewart Butterfield's obsession with details wasn't natural talent — it was a discipline."
6. **Signpost:** "That's all 7 concepts. You now have the complete product strategy toolkit — from what strategy is, to how to craft one, prioritize it, defend it, and develop the underlying skill that makes you better at all of it. Let's wrap up."

## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet
- **The Kernel** (Richard Rumelt): Good strategy = diagnosis + guiding policy + coherent actions. Bad strategy = goals dressed up as strategy, fluff, or incoherent actions.
- **Strategy Choice Cascade** (Roger Martin): Five questions — winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities, management systems. You must be differentiated or low cost.
- **7 Powers** (Hamilton Helmer): Seven sources of durable competitive advantage — counter-positioning, scale economies, switching costs, network economies, branding, process power, cornered resource. Power = benefit + barrier.
- **DHM Model** (Gibson Biddle / Netflix): Delight customers in Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing ways. Strategy hypotheses tested through A/B experiments.
- **Strategy Blocks** (Chandra Janakiraman): Small "s" strategy (2-year, problem-forward, 5 steps in 8-12 weeks) + Big "S" strategy (3-10 year, future-backward). Score opportunity areas on impact, certainty, clarity of levers, uniqueness of levers.
- **Mission → Vision → Strategy → Goals → Roadmap → Task** (Lenny Rachitsky): The full hierarchy from purpose to daily work. Strategy is your plan to win; it sits between vision and goals.
- **RICE / DRICE** (Darius Contractor & Alexey Komissarouk): RICE = T-shirt sizing on Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort. DRICE = 30-minute deep dive per idea producing dollar estimates. Teams using DRICE saw 2x impact rate at Dropbox.
- **Cover Fire** (Lenny Rachitsky): 80% short-term wins, 20% big bets. Ship incremental results to earn the right to take long-term risks.
- **Now-Next-Later** (Janna Bastow): Roadmap as a prototype for your strategy — lay out assumptions about problems to solve, not date-based commitments.
- **Product Sense** (Jules Walter): Empathy (discovering user needs) + Creativity (effective solutions). Built through 4 practices: observe users, deconstruct products, learn from great thinkers, track technology shifts.
- **Vision Storytelling** (Ebi Atawodi): Once upon a time → Because of that → One day → Because of that. Vision must be lofty, realistic, free of today's limitations, and grounded in a user problem.
