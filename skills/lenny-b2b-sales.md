# /lenny-b2b-sales - B2B Sales & GTM Mastery

From zero to your first customers, founder-led sales to a repeatable GTM engine — the complete playbook for B2B builders who want to close deals, not just build products.

## About This Journey
Most B2B founders build something real and then struggle to sell it. This journey pulls the hard-won lessons from Pete Kazanjy, Jen Abel, April Dunford, Matt Dixon, Geoffrey Moore, Elena Verna, Jason Lemkin, Jeanne DeWitt Grosser, and dozens of founder case studies (Figma, Gong, Okta, Notion, Retool, Zip, and more) into a single sequential curriculum. You'll go from ICP definition through your first 10 customers, positioning and pitch, closing the deal, and eventually scaling a repeatable GTM motion — whether sales-led, product-led, or a hybrid.

Best for: Early-stage B2B founders or product leaders at seed to Series A who are doing founder-led sales or preparing to hand off their first sales hires, selling anything from $5K to $500K ACV.

## The Curriculum

1. **ICP First, Always** — Why the sharpest companies start with a narrow Ideal Customer Profile and how to define yours with precision before writing a single email. *(Sources: "How to identify your ideal customer profile (ICP)", Pete Kazanjy — Founder-led sales)*

2. **Finding Your First 10 Customers** — The three tactics every top B2B startup used (personal network, strategic outbound, community) and why none of them scale — which is exactly why they work. *(Sources: "How to find and win your first 10 B2B customers", "How today's fastest growing B2B businesses found their first ten customers")*

3. **Founder-Led Sales as Learning, Not Revenue** — Why the founder must sell first, how to use early sales calls to learn instead of close, and what signals tell you it's time to hire a salesperson. *(Sources: Pete Kazanjy — Founder-led sales, Jen Abel — Sell the alpha)*

4. **Positioning and the Sales Pitch** — April Dunford's framework for why "we're better" never works, how to frame against the real competition (no decision, not a competitor), and the two-part sales pitch structure that wins. *(Sources: "How to build a killer sales pitch", April Dunford — Product positioning)*

5. **Closing Without Pressure: The JOLT Method** — Matt Dixon's research from 2.5M sales calls: why 40–60% of B2B deals die to indecision, why FOMO tactics backfire 87% of the time, and how the JOLT method de-risks the deal for the buyer. *(Sources: Matt Dixon — The surprising truth about what closes deals)*

6. **Choosing Your GTM Motion: Sales-Led, PLG, or Hybrid** — The GTM motions of 30 top B2B SaaS companies, when to go product-led, when to layer on sales, and Elena Verna's framework for product-led sales. *(Sources: "GTM motions of 30 B2B SaaS companies", Elena Verna — B2B growth and PLG, Hila Qu — Adding a PLG motion)*

7. **Scaling the Growth Engine** — When to hire your first salesperson (and what profile to look for), how Jeanne DeWitt Grosser's GTM-as-a-product model works, and what the six B2B growth channels look like in practice. *(Sources: "Scaling your B2B growth engine", Jeanne DeWitt Grosser — World-class GTM, Jason Lemkin — Replacing sales teams with AI)*

### Concept-Specific Teaching Notes

**Concept 1 — ICP First, Always**
Load: `newsletters/how-to-identify-your-ideal-customer-profile-icp.md`

Key insight to teach: ICP has two layers — the account (what company) and the personas inside it (who you talk to, who uses it, who owns the budget). As Pete Kazanjy puts it, "If you're not crisp around who has your problem and why, you're wasting time on people who don't have your problem." The ICP isn't just a targeting filter — it's the foundation of your message, your outbound, and your product roadmap. Teach the difference between account-level ICP (size, industry, stage, tech stack) and persona-level ICP (user, champion, economic buyer — often three different people).

Check understanding question: "For your product, who is the account-level ICP — what does a company need to look like to be a real candidate? And then inside that company, who actually uses it, who champions it, and who signs the check?"

**Concept 2 — Finding Your First 10 Customers**
Load: `newsletters/how-to-find-and-win-your-first-10-b2b-customers.md`

Key insight to teach: Every top B2B startup (Figma, Gong, Okta, Notion, Gusto, Retool, Zip) used one of three tactics to find their first 10 customers: personal network, strategic cold outbound, and community participation. As Lenny's research shows, "none of these seven strategies scale — that's why they work. In B2B, it always starts with hand-to-hand combat." Highlight Retool's approach (filtered Crunchbase by fundraising date and vertical, emailed CTO and VP of Ops) and Zip's approach (LinkedIn cold outreach framed as asking for advice, not selling). Teach Jen Abel's rule: before any tool, can you manually find 30 people you'd spend 15–20 minutes writing a thoughtful note to? If you can't find 30, that's your first signal.

Check understanding question: "Using your ICP from concept one, what's the first tactic you'd use to find your first 10 — and who specifically would you reach out to first?"

**Concept 3 — Founder-Led Sales as Learning, Not Revenue**
Load: `podcasts/jen-abel.md`
Secondary: `podcasts/pete-kazanjy.md`

Key insight to teach: As Jen Abel says directly, "Founder-led sales is not about revenue on day one. It is about learning as fast as humanly possible to get to that pulse, so that you can earn the right to sell." Teach two counterintuitive moves: (1) Be vulnerable about being early-stage — "I'm an early-stage startup, we have a lot to learn, can we gain your insight?" gets more honest feedback than pretending to be further along. (2) Avoid the word "better" — "better means something different to everyone" and triggers procurement to ask you to prove it. Lead with a counterintuitive insight or a problem framing that makes the buyer say "wait, what?" Pete Kazanjy's "while loop" concept: before hiring a salesperson, you need a statistically significant win rate (~15–25%) across 50–100 attempts that reliably repeats. That's the signal to hand off to someone else.

Check understanding question: "What's the counterintuitive insight or problem framing at the core of your product — the thing that would make a prospect say 'wait, how is that possible?' in your cold outreach?"

**Concept 4 — Positioning and the Sales Pitch**
Load: `newsletters/how-to-build-a-killer-sales-pitch.md`
Secondary: `podcasts/april-dunford.md`

Key insight to teach: April Dunford's central argument: "40 to 60% of B2B purchase processes end in 'no decision' — not because the buyer chose a competitor, but because they couldn't figure out how to confidently make a decision." Your real competition isn't another product, it's the buyer's default of doing nothing. This changes the pitch entirely. Teach Dunford's two-part pitch structure: Part 1 sets up the "problem landscape" — here's the world, here's why the status quo is broken, here's what buyers who've escaped it look like. Part 2 is your product's role: not "here's what we do" but "here's how you become one of the winners in part 1." Positioning also means defining what you are NOT — as Dunford puts it, positioning starts with the alternatives, not with your features.

Check understanding question: "For your product, what is the 'no decision' story — what does the buyer do instead of buying you, and what does it cost them to stay there?"

**Concept 5 — Closing Without Pressure: The JOLT Method**
Load: `podcasts/matt-dixon.md`

Key insight to teach: Matt Dixon's research from 2.5 million sales calls found that 56% of no-decision losses come from buyers who want to buy but can't — they're stuck in indecision driven by fear of failure, not status quo preference. The key insight: "Dialing up the FOMO backfires 87% of the time. They're not afraid of missing out, they're afraid of messing up." Teach the JOLT method: **J**udge their level of indecision (use "pings and echoes" — surface what's blocking them without making them feel accused of being indecisive). **O**ffer a recommendation (narrow choices, don't present options). **L**imit the exploration (stop letting them do endless research). **T**ake risk off the table (de-risk the downside, help them feel they'll look like a hero not a fool). The Challenger Sale insight connects here: show customers what should be keeping them up at night, not just what is.

Check understanding question: "Think about a deal you've lost or are currently stuck on. Based on what you just learned — was it more likely status quo preference (they actually preferred doing nothing) or fear of failure (they wanted to buy but couldn't commit)? How would you diagnose which one it was?"

**Concept 6 — Choosing Your GTM Motion: Sales-Led, PLG, or Hybrid**
Load: `newsletters/gtm-motions-of-30-b2b-saas-companies.md`
Secondary: `podcasts/elena-verna-30.md` and `podcasts/hila-qu.md`

Key insight to teach: Lenny's research on 30 top B2B SaaS companies shows there's no single right motion — but there is a predictable evolution. Companies like Figma, Datadog, and Airtable started product-led (bottom-up, freemium, self-serve), while Workday, Snowflake, and Salesforce started sales-led (top-down, outbound). The key insight: "100% of the bottom-up B2B companies ended up layering on a sales team. It's a question of when, not if." Elena Verna's framework: PLG creates a PQL (product-qualified lead) — a user whose behavior signals buying intent. Product-led sales is when your sales team acts on those PQLs rather than doing cold outbound. Hila Qu's warning: "PLG is fundamentally DLG — data-led growth. If you don't have the data infrastructure to understand which free users convert and why, you're giving away your product for nothing."

Check understanding question: "Looking at your product today — is your buyer the same person as your user? If yes, PLG is likely viable. If no (e.g. an IT buyer purchases what a developer or designer uses), what does that tell you about which motion to prioritize first?"

**Concept 7 — Scaling the Growth Engine**
Load: `newsletters/scaling-your-b2b-growth-engine.md`
Secondary: `podcasts/jeanne-grosser.md`

Key insight to teach: Lenny's research shows the biggest growth channel for scaling B2B companies is organic inbound (word of mouth), but that sales eventually becomes necessary for every company regardless. Teach the six B2B growth channels: self-serve inbound (PLG), sales-assist inbound, outbound sales, content/SEO, paid ads, and partnerships — and the rule "most of your growth will come from one of the top three." On hiring the first salesperson: the signal is a repeatable win rate (Pete Kazanjy's "while loop"), not a calendar date. Wait until you have a clear ICP, a documented pitch, and can describe what success looks like to the hire. Jeanne DeWitt Grosser's framework: think of GTM as a product — you run sprints, fix bugs in your sales process, and treat lost deals as data. Her "deal-bot" example: AI agents running on Gong transcripts to catch why deals really lost (often not what the AE reported).

Check understanding question: "For your stage right now, which of the six growth channels are you relying on most — and what's the one adjacent channel worth experimenting with next?"

## Key Frameworks Cheat Sheet

- **ICP Two-Layer Model** (Pete Kazanjy): Account-level ICP (what company) + Persona-level ICP (user, champion, economic buyer — often three different people). Most founders only do the first layer.

- **The Sales While Loop** (Pete Kazanjy): Before hiring your first salesperson, achieve a statistically repeatable win rate of 15–25% across 50–100 attempts. This proves the motion is packageable and teachable. "It's not 'if' you hire a salesperson, it's 'when' — and the trigger is repeatability, not a calendar date."

- **Founder-Led Sales = Learning, Not Revenue** (Jen Abel): "Founder-led sales is not about revenue on day one. It is about learning as fast as humanly possible to get to that pulse, so that you can earn the right to sell." Sales for research → Sales for revenue is the two-stage model.

- **The Four-Part Cold Outreach Formula** (Jen Abel): (1) Relevancy — why are you reaching out to this specific person now? (2) Counterintuitive or surprising framing — something that makes them say "wait, what?" (3) Problem focus, not solution focus — don't mention what you built, mention the problem. (4) Concise — fits on a mobile screen without scrolling.

- **Real Competition = No Decision** (April Dunford): "40–60% of B2B purchase processes end in no decision. The buyer didn't choose a competitor — they couldn't figure out how to confidently choose anything." The pitch must address the switching cost and the status quo problem, not just the feature comparison.

- **The Two-Part Sales Pitch Structure** (April Dunford): Part 1 — set up the problem landscape (why the status quo is broken, what winners look like). Part 2 — position your product as the vehicle to become one of the winners. Never lead with features; lead with the world they want to live in.

- **FOMU > FOMO** (Matt Dixon, The JOLT Effect): Buyers are not afraid of missing out — they're afraid of messing up. "Dialing up the FOMO backfires 87% of the time." The fear of being personally blamed for a bad purchase decision (omission bias) is more powerful than status quo bias.

- **JOLT Method** (Matt Dixon): **J**udge their indecision level → **O**ffer a recommendation (narrow choices) → **L**imit the exploration (stop the endless research loop) → **T**ake risk off the table (de-risk the downside, make them look like a hero).

- **The Challenger Sale** (Matt Dixon): "Most salespeople try to figure out what's keeping the customer up at night. The challenger approach shows the customer what should be keeping them up at night — a risk they don't know about but you do." Challenge the buyer's worldview; don't just respond to it.

- **Technology Adoption Lifecycle and the Chasm** (Geoffrey Moore): Visionaries buy on vision ("we believe what you believe"). Pragmatists buy on peer references ("we need what you have"). The chasm is the gap between them — pragmatists won't accept a visionary as a reference. The fix: win a beachhead segment (same industry, geography, profession) deeply enough that pragmatists see their peers using it.

- **Beachhead Formula** (Geoffrey Moore): "Big enough to matter, small enough to lead, and a good fit with your crown jewels." Win one segment completely before expanding to adjacent ones (the bowling alley model).

- **PLG Motion = DLG (Data-Led Growth)** (Hila Qu): "When you give away a free product, what you want in exchange are two things: broader reach and usage behavior data. If you don't have the data infrastructure to understand which free users convert and why, you are giving away your product for nothing."

- **Product-Led Sales** (Elena Verna): PLG generates PQLs (product-qualified leads) based on usage signals. Product-led sales is when your sales team acts on those PQLs with precision — combining the distribution benefits of PLG with the expansion power of a sales motion.

- **GTM as a Product** (Jeanne DeWitt Grosser): Treat go-to-market like an engineering system — map workflows, identify bugs (lost deals, objection gaps), run sprints to fix them, ship improvements weekly. "Sellers spend only 30–40% of their time in front of customers. The rest is admin. AI agents can change that ratio dramatically."

- **Six B2B Growth Channels** (Lenny's research): (1) Organic inbound, self-serve (PLG), (2) Organic inbound, sales-assist, (3) Outbound sales, (4) Content/SEO, (5) Paid ads, (6) Partnerships. Most of your growth at scale comes from one of the first three. The other three are amplifiers, not foundations.

- **Hand-to-Hand Combat Rule** (Lenny's research across Figma, Gong, Retool, Zip, Okta): "None of the seven strategies for finding early customers scale. That's why they work. In B2B, it always starts with hand-to-hand combat." Personal network, creative outbound, and community are the only tactics that work before you have references and a brand.
